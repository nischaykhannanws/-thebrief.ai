Lead · Forex Reserves · March Stress

India's forex reserves shed $30.5 billion in March — the RBI's biggest single-month intervention in memory, and the rupee still ended Asia's worst performer of the year.

Well, you see, the numbers are striking. India's forex reserves sat at a record $728.5 billion in the week ending February 27. By the week ending March 27, they had fallen to $688.06 billion — a $30.5 billion drain in a single month. The RBI sold approximately $16 billion in foreign currency assets directly to defend the rupee as FPIs pulled out $12 billion from Indian equities and bond markets fled for exits. The rupee hit a low of ₹94.85 on March 27, down over 4% in the month alone — Asia's worst-performing major currency in FY26.

Not only this — the RBI's most dramatic intervention in over a decade, capping bank open FX positions at $100 million per day from March 27, brought only a fleeting 1.4% jump before the rupee gave it all back. With oil marketing companies buying $250–300 million daily and FPIs unwinding positions, the structural pressure hasn't eased. Import cover stands at roughly 11 months at spot, but adjusting for the RBI's $100 billion net short forward position, it narrows to 9.4 months. Due to this, the next MPC statement on April 8 is being watched as closely as any market event this quarter.

Lead · The Economist · Iran War Day 34

An American fighter jet shot down over Iranian airspace — one crew member rescued, one still missing. The Economist flags this as the clearest escalation trigger the war has produced yet.

Well, you see, this is the scenario that changes the diplomatic calculus overnight. The Economist's lead story on April 3 flagged the downed US fighter jet as a potential watershed: if Iran captures the second airman alive, Trump faces enormous domestic pressure to escalate beyond what the air campaign has already done. His stated playbook — "obliterate" Iranian power plants, bomb them "back to the stone ages" — suddenly moves from rhetorical to operational territory. The White House has not confirmed the full situation publicly, but the story was The Economist's lead and was flagged as a potential escalation trigger above anything seen in the prior 34 days of war.

Not only this — the war has produced a parallel geopolitical realignment that The Economist tracked in its most-read piece of the week. China is quietly positioning itself to benefit from the conflict: it has been receiving Iranian and Chinese-flagged tankers through the Strait, deepening ties with both Iran and the Gulf states seeking alternatives to US security guarantees, and positioning itself as the neutral broker as Qatar and Oman push for negotiation. Russia is simultaneously filling supply vacuums in India and China, seeing its crude exports and geopolitical influence both expand. Due to this, the US is technically winning militarily but strategically creating openings for both Beijing and Moscow.
