Aftermarket Report · Tuesday 24 Mar 2026 · Nifty Expiry Day

Nifty reclaims 22,900 on Nifty expiry day — but by evening, fresh strikes resumed and oil climbed back above $100

Lead · Aftermarket Report · Tuesday · Nifty Expiry

366-point gap-up, a dip to 22,650, then a steady grind higher to test 23,050. Nifty closed at 22,912 (+1.78%). All 12 sectors green. But after the close, Iranian missiles hit Tel Aviv, oil climbed back above $100, and the 5-day pause looked shakier by the hour.

You see, today was Nifty weekly expiry day — and it behaved exactly like one. The gap-up open at 22,878 on Trump's Iran pause news was followed by the predictable profit-booking and early morning dip (touching 22,650 by 10:30 AM), before buying interest picked up steadily through the day, pushing Nifty to intraday highs near 23,050 around 1:45 PM. After that, the index turned range-bound between 22,900 and 22,980 as gains were capped near resistance. A mild bout of profit-booking in the final hour dragged it slightly lower. Final close: 22,912 (+1.78%). All 12 sectors closed in green — Media led (+3.45%), Energy was the least positive (+0.70%). 2,483 stocks advanced vs 809 declined.

Not only this — today's recovery came in stark contrast to Monday's bloodbath, where every sector was in the red. The broader market turnaround (2,483 advances vs 809 declines today, compared to 332 advances vs 3,008 declines yesterday) shows how directly the market has tied itself to Iran ceasefire signals. Due to this, the single most important variable for markets this week remains not macro data, not earnings — it is the Iran talks. And those talks just got more complicated: by the time you're reading this, Iranian missiles struck Tel Aviv, an Israeli official said a deal "does not appear to be tangible right now," and oil climbed back above $100. The ceasefire window is not closed — but it is no longer the confident story it was yesterday afternoon.

4 stories
Lead · ETAuto · Tuesday

India's strategic oil reserves are two-thirds full — but Saudi Aramco is cutting Asia supply for the second consecutive month in April

You see, these two stories need to be read together. India has been filling its strategic petroleum reserves (SPRs) — three underground caverns at Visakhapatnam, Mangaluru, and Padur — and the Minister confirmed today they are approximately two-thirds full. On paper, this is reassuring. The government also approved commercialisation of Phase-I SPRs (allowing the stored oil to be lent to refiners and recovered later), creating a buffer mechanism for supply disruptions.

Now read that against Saudi Aramco cutting Asia-bound oil supply for the second consecutive month in April. Saudi Arabia was already running at a fiscal deficit when oil was below $91 — and with Hormuz effectively closed to Gulf exports, Aramco is allocating whatever it can still ship through alternative routes to preferred customers first. India, which sources roughly 15-18% of its crude from Saudi Arabia, is not in that preferred tier. Not only does this compound the Hormuz supply crunch — it means India's oil import alternatives are narrowing even from the routes that bypass Hormuz.

Daily Brief · Finshots · The Ken

The petrodollar's quiet death, HDFC Bank's three unanswered questions, and finding disease before it finds you

4 reads
Lead · The Daily Brief · Zerodha · Tuesday

The petrodollar was the most powerful structural prop of dollar dominance for 50 years. Brad Setser says it has "more or less dried up." And the Hormuz crisis doesn't work the way the 1973 playbook says it should.

You see, the conventional assumption about a Hormuz closure is that it triggers a classic petrodollar moment: oil prices spike, Gulf exporters rake in dollars, those dollars flow into US Treasury bonds, and dollar dominance is reinforced. Zerodha's Daily Brief takes this assumption apart — methodically and brilliantly — using Brad Setser's research on the petrodollar's structural decline.

Here's the argument: in 1973, Saudi Arabia and other Gulf states had nowhere to park their oil profits except US Treasuries — giving the US cheap borrowing and giving the Gulf a productive outlet for its dollar surplus. That deal held for decades. But then Gulf states built sovereign wealth funds. Saudi's PIF, UAE's ADIA, Kuwait's KIA — they collectively control $4–5 trillion in assets, with ~70% in equities, not fixed income. They're buying Apple and Nvidia, not US Treasuries. The petrodollar recycling loop has effectively broken down.

Not only this — in a Hormuz closure, the Gulf states whose exports are physically blocked (Iraq, Kuwait, Qatar, UAE) aren't generating dollar surpluses at all. The real winners are Russia, Kazakhstan, Azerbaijan, and Norway — exporters whose routes bypass Hormuz. But none of those countries recycle dollars into US Treasuries the way the 1970s playbook assumed. Meanwhile, the biggest losers are India, Japan, and South Korea — countries whose energy import bills balloon while their dollar surpluses shrink. India is literally selling its forex reserves to keep the rupee from collapsing. The full Daily Brief piece is the best macro read this week — it's also the frame that makes the LPG cylinder story and the rupee story and the India 10Y yield story all make sense simultaneously.

Daily Brief · World Water Day
India exports 50 billion m³ of virtual water per year through rice — and Punjab borewells now need to go 200 feet to find water
The Daily Brief's second piece today (on World Water Day, 22 March) is a World Bank report analysis that reframes India's water crisis as a mispricing problem, not just a scarcity problem. India exports roughly 20 million tonnes of rice annually — and producing 1 kg of rice in India requires 3,000–4,000 litres of water. So India is effectively exporting ~50 billion m³ of water per year in rice form. In Punjab, groundwater extraction runs at 150% of natural recharge. Borewells that hit water at 30 feet a decade ago now need 200 feet. The cause: government guarantees paddy procurement at rising prices while providing free, unmetered electricity to 14 lakh tubewells. Every individual farmer is rational — but the collective outcome is aquifer depletion. The Daily Brief compares this with Georgia's successful water institution reform and asks what it would take to fix India. Essential reading.

The Ken · Health Tech
Shashank ND (Practo founder) built a startup to find doctors — now he's built one to find disease before the doctors do
Cent is Shashank ND's new startup: AI-aided early disease detection using an orchestration layer that aggregates data from Siemens medical equipment (MRI, CT, DEXA scans). For ₹20,000–30,000, you get a comprehensive results report. The catch: Cent provides results but neither diagnoses nor treatment plans — those require a doctor. The Ken's story by Sudeshna Ray asks the harder question: what is the healthcare pathway for someone Cent tells "something may be wrong" in a country where GPs have no financial incentive to treat patients who aren't yet seriously sick? India's primary healthcare system is not built for proactive follow-up. Due to this gap, the value proposition of early detection depends on a downstream healthcare pathway that doesn't reliably exist. The reliance on pre-existing Siemens tech and a shortage of India-specific disease data are the two limitations the story highlights. Well worth reading for anyone in health tech or health policy.

The Economist · Iran War Day 25

Trump's climbdown, Iran's defiance, and the 5-day window — what today's fresh strikes mean

2 reads
Breaking · Day 25 · Multiple Sources · Tuesday 24 March

The 5-day pause is being tested: Iran acknowledges US "outreach" through mediators — but Iranian missiles hit Tel Aviv today and oil climbed back above $100

The diplomatic picture today is more ambiguous than it was yesterday. On the positive side: a senior Iranian FM official told CBS News that "we received points from the US through mediators and they are being reviewed" — the first acknowledgement from Tehran that US-Iran contact exists, even indirectly. Pakistan offered to host formal talks. Turkey's FM spoke to over a dozen counterparts in 48 hours. An Iranian source told CNN that Tehran is willing to listen to "sustainable" proposals and is ready to provide guarantees it will never build nuclear weapons, provided sanctions are lifted and the war ends.

On the negative side: Iranian missiles struck multiple areas of Tel Aviv today causing significant building damage. An Israeli official said a deal "does not appear to be tangible right now." Iran's IRGC called Trump a "deceitful American president" and accused him of "contradictory behaviour." Iran also named a new Supreme National Security Council secretary (Mohammad Bagher Zolghadr) after the previous one was killed in strikes. Oil prices climbed back above $100/bbl after the fresh fighting diminished ceasefire hopes. Not only this — the IEA warned that the current energy crisis is "worse than the oil shocks of the 1970s." The five-day window expires Saturday. The direction of the next 72 hours will be decisive.
