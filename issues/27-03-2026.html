Friday closed with the week's worst session. Nifty at 22,819, rupee at a record low, and an excise cut that won't actually cut fuel prices. Here's everything that happened.

Lead · Aftermarket Report · Friday 27 March 2026

Nifty opened gap-down 132 points on Friday and never recovered — closing at 22,819.60, down ~486 points (-2.1%). All 12 sectors red. 503 advances vs 2,813 declines. PSU Bank the worst at -3.86%. And the rupee hit a record low past ₹94.7 per dollar. This is the week's full verdict.

You see, the market had been nursing a two-day recovery rally into Thursday's holiday. The Wednesday close at 23,306 (+1.72%) felt like the corner had been turned. Then Friday arrived — gap-down, selling through the morning, break below 23,000 in the first hour, further slide to 22,950 by late morning, and final collapse to 22,819 by close. There was no meaningful buying support at any level. The session was defined by what the Aftermarket Report called "sustained selling and a lack of meaningful buying" — which is market-speak for everyone heading for the exits at once.

The macro triggers were stacking up. Trump extended the Iran deadline by 10 days to April 6 — which should have been a relief signal, but WTI crude climbed back to ~$97 because markets didn't believe the extension would produce a deal. The rupee broke past ₹94.7/dollar, heading for its worst fiscal-year decline in over a decade, driven by persistent capital outflows and the energy crisis. The government's ₹10/litre excise duty cut on petrol and diesel — politically significant — came with a technical asterisk: retail prices won't fall because the cut is designed to cover OMC underrecoveries, not to pass savings to consumers. And Reliance fell 4%+ after the government simultaneously levied an export duty on petrol/diesel — effectively capping the refining margin upside that Reliance had been benefiting from.

Policy · Fuel
Govt cuts excise duty on petrol/diesel by ₹10/litre — but your pump price won't change. Here's why that isn't a contradiction.
The ₹10/litre cut sounds like relief, but it's really an accounting adjustment. OMCs have been selling petrol and diesel below import cost since the war began — the government has been covering those underrecoveries through subsidy transfers. The excise cut simply formalises that support into the duty structure. No money flows to consumers; it flows to the oil companies to keep them from bleeding further. Retail prices remain sticky until crude falls structurally. The political optics are strong — the headline number ₹10/litre is large. The economic reality is more muted. Stocks · Reliance
Reliance fell 4%+ after govt levied export duty on petrol/diesel. The refining margin trade is now off the table — for now.
Reliance's Jamnagar refinery had been one of the biggest indirect beneficiaries of the Iran oil shock — high crack spreads meant strong refining margins, and the company had been exporting refined products at elevated global prices. The government's new export duty directly caps that upside. When a refiner can no longer freely export at international parity prices, the margin gets compressed back toward domestic regulated levels. Not only this — Reliance also had to formally deny reports of purchasing Iranian-origin crude (confirmed separately by Screener.in watchlist update), calling the claims "false and misleading." The stock fell sharply on both announcements. Source: Aftermarket Report.

ETAuto · Friday 27 Mar 2026

MHI wants your car running on natural gas. Delhi's buses are losing the city. And Bajaj just posted €590 million profit from Europe.

3 stories
Lead · ETAuto · Friday 27 March 2026

The Ministry of Heavy Industries has now pushed natural gas as an interim fuel strategy for the auto sector — a significant pivot from Thursday's electricity-only message. Meanwhile, Delhi's public bus system has quietly lost 20% of its ridership since FY20, and Bajaj's European mobility arm posted €590 million in net profit, reminding India who actually leads the global 2W export game.

You see, the government's energy messaging to the auto sector has now acquired a more nuanced shape. Thursday it was "shift to electricity." Friday it is "shift to natural gas as a bridge." This isn't a contradiction — it's a recognition that the electricity infrastructure transition will take years, and that natural gas (CNG, LNG for commercial vehicles) can serve as a lower-emission interim fuel while the grid and charging network catch up. Not only this — the Iran oil shock has made the case for any non-crude-oil fuel significantly stronger. Natural gas feedstock for Indian city buses and commercial vehicles can be sourced domestically or from pipeline-connected suppliers who aren't blocked by the Hormuz closure.

The Delhi bus ridership story is the quieter crisis. A 20% decline since FY20 in a city that desperately needs its buses to work — and is now building green budgets around them — is a structural problem. It points to reliability, frequency, and route coverage failures that no amount of EV bus procurement can fix on its own. And then there's Bajaj Mobility posting €590 million net profit in 2025 from its European operations — a number that puts in stark relief just how valuable the global export opportunity is for Indian 2W manufacturers who have been playing it right.

Policy · Fuel Transition
MHI pushes natural gas as auto sector's interim fuel — a two-track strategy: gas for the bridge, electricity for the destination
The Ministry of Heavy Industries' natural gas push is best understood as a layer added to — not replacing — the electrification direction. Natural gas vehicles include CNG cars and buses (already widely deployed in India's cities), LNG-powered heavy trucks (growing segment for highway freight), and PNG-fuelled industrial vehicles in port and logistics facilities. The case for gas as a bridge: India has its own gas production plus long-term LNG import contracts with countries outside the Hormuz zone (Australia, US, Qatar — though Qatar is partly Hormuz-dependent). It burns cleaner than diesel (lower PM2.5 and NOx), the infrastructure already exists in major cities, and the technology is mature. Due to the oil shock, CNG has now become significantly cheaper per kilometre than petrol for equivalent vehicles. The counter-argument: natural gas is still a fossil fuel, and any large-scale scale-up of gas vehicle infrastructure risks creating a new stranded asset problem when EV penetration finally tips. That said, the MHI position appears to be acknowledging that "pure EV now" is impractical for the commercial vehicle segment, particularly for long-haul freight where battery range and charging infrastructure are still inadequate. Urban Mobility · Delhi
Delhi bus ridership has dropped 20% since FY20 — in the same city that just announced a ₹22,236 crore green budget built around buses and metro
A 20 per cent ridership decline over five years is not a small number. In absolute terms, Delhi's bus network carried around 4 million passengers daily in FY20 — a 20% decline means roughly 800,000 fewer daily trips that have moved to private vehicles, autos, or simply gone unmade. The reasons are compounding: fleet age (many Delhi Transport Corporation buses are over 10 years old and unreliable), frequency gaps on non-metro corridors, overcrowding on popular routes, safety concerns, and the competitive rise of app-based autos and bike taxis that now offer cheaper, faster last-mile alternatives. Not only this — the bus network has not kept pace with Delhi's physical expansion into new residential zones in the north, east, and south. The green budget announced this week allocates significant funds to EV buses — but the deeper problem is operational: frequency, reliability, and integration with metro last-mile. Due to the Iran oil shock, there is a case that bus economics are improving (CNG is cheaper than petrol on a per-km basis now), but that alone won't bring riders back without service improvement. Is Iran running a ₹16 crore/ship toll booth? Why your apps are getting worse on purpose. And how Rapido is trying to make you pay ₹567 for biryani instead of ₹847.

3 reads
Lead · Daily Brief (Zerodha) · Friday 27 March 2026

You have been following the Iran war for 28 days now. But today's Daily Brief asks a question that changes the entire framing: is Iran not just blocking the Strait of Hormuz — is it charging ships $2 million each to pass through it? And if it is, that changes everything about what a "ceasefire" would even mean.

Well, let's find out. Twenty per cent of the world's oil passes through a 21-mile-wide strip of water between Iran and Oman. For decades, that passage was free — guaranteed by international law, enforced by the US Navy. Back in the day, war risk insurance for a ship transiting the Strait cost roughly $40,000–$100,000 for a large oil tanker. Put simply, it was the cheapest part of moving oil across the planet. Then the war began, and that number surged to approximately $5 million per transit — just insurance, before the cargo, the crew, or the ship.

That context is what makes the Daily Brief's Hormuz toll story so important. Reports from Bloomberg, Lloyd's List, and the Financial Times suggest Iran is now charging ships up to $2 million to pass through a corridor between Qeshm and Larak islands, supervised by the IRGC. Iranian parliamentarians confirm it on state TV. Iran's embassy in India calls it "unfounded." Iran's Foreign Ministry won't comment. The ambiguity may not be a bug — it may be a feature. A formal toll invites legal challenges and gives the US a cleaner justification for military action. An informal one lets Iran collect the money while maintaining diplomatic deniability. And for India — which imports 45% of its crude through the strait — the difference between a $2 million toll and a $5 million insurance bill is not academic. It is the difference between a manageable cost and a structural tax on the entire economy.

Finshots · Platforms
Finshots: Why do apps get worse the more you use them? Zomato's platform fee is up 645% in under three years. This is not an accident. It has a name: enshittification.
You opened Zomato last week and noticed the platform fee had quietly gone up again. To ₹14.90. Then Swiggy matched it at ₹17.58. You paid, because what else would you do — call the restaurant directly like it's 2015? That, exactly, is the point. Finshots today explains a framework called enshittification — coined in 2022 by writer Cory Doctorow — which describes why platforms inevitably decay. Phase one: the platform subsidises users to build scale (cheap delivery, deep discounts, VC money funding the gap). Phase two: once users are locked in, it begins favouring business customers — advertisers, brand partners — at users' expense. Phase three: it extracts from everyone — users and business customers both — to deliver shareholder returns. Each stage leaves just enough value for people not to walk away. Nothing more. In India, the enshittification problem has a structural amplifier: almost every consumer-facing market settles into a duopoly. Jio–Airtel. IndiGo–Air India. Amazon–Flipkart. Swiggy–Zomato. In a duopoly, interoperability (the primary fix Doctorow proposes) requires both players to agree, and neither has any incentive to. Source: Finshots.

Daily Brief (Zerodha) · Multiple sources · Friday 27 March 2026

The Hormuz toll: fact, bluff, or both? A deep read on why a $2mn/ship fee might be the most important geopolitical question of the week — and what it means for India.

Deep Read
Deep Read · Daily Brief (Zerodha) · Friday 27 March 2026

Back in the day, the Strait of Hormuz operated under a simple rule: free passage, guaranteed by international law, policed by the US Navy. Today, Iran may be sitting on the world's most valuable chokepoint and charging $2 million per ship for the privilege of using it. Here's the full story — the legal question, the economics, the diplomatic fog, and India's precarious position in the middle of all of it.

Well, you see, the Hormuz toll story exists simultaneously in two forms. The first is an informal, operational system: ships are being routed through a narrow corridor between Iran's Qeshm and Larak islands, passing through Iranian territorial waters under IRGC supervision. According to Lloyd's List, at least two vessels reportedly paid around $2 million each for passage. The second is a formal bill reportedly moving through Iran's parliament — drafted to legally enshrine the toll, giving Iran a permanent legal instrument for Hormuz transit fees. The ambiguity between the two is almost certainly intentional. Formally acknowledging the toll would invite ICJ challenges and give the US a cleaner casus belli for military action against Iran's naval assets. Informally collecting it maintains plausible deniability while still generating revenue.

Can Iran actually do this legally? Simply put, no. The UN Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS) is unambiguous: all ships enjoy the right of "transit passage" through international straits, and no fee may be levied for that passage alone. Iran signed but never ratified UNCLOS — which gives it a narrow technical argument. But international law scholars regard transit passage as customary international law, binding on all states regardless of treaty ratification. The International Court of Justice established this principle in 1949, three decades before UNCLOS existed. Iranian lawmakers have cited Suez, Panama, and the Bosphorus as precedents — but each of those actually undermines the argument: Suez and Panama are man-made canals with infrastructure costs to recover; Turkey's Bosphorus charges modest fees under a 1936 treaty grandfathered into modern maritime law. Hormuz is a natural waterway with no infrastructure and no treaty. That said, in a war, legal rights are only as enforceable as the power behind them. And the US Navy has destroyed 130+ Iranian naval vessels and still cannot guarantee safe passage.

The economics create a perverse logic: the toll may be cheaper than the alternative. Pre-war, a Very Large Crude Carrier (VLCC) transiting Hormuz paid $40,000–$100,000 in insurance. By mid-March 2026, that number hit ~$5 million per transit. Charter rates quadrupled. Total additional cost per voyage reached $4–6 million. In that context, $2 million for guaranteed safe passage — with the IRGC actively escorting your vessel — starts to look like a bargain. Iran knows this. The revenue potential at scale is enormous: 138 ships per day pre-crisis, carrying 20 million barrels of oil. An Iranian newspaper proposed a 10% toll could generate $73 billion a year — wildly theoretical, but it reveals the scale of Iran's ambition. Due to this, the toll is best understood not as an act of war but as a bargaining chip. Iran's ceasefire conditions include lifting all sanctions, international legal guarantees, and — crucially — establishing a "new legal regime" for the strait. The toll gives Iran something concrete to trade away at the negotiating table.

India · Hormuz Exposure
India is arguably the most exposed major economy to the Hormuz crisis — and it's navigating it one tanker at a time through quiet bilateral diplomacy with Tehran
India imports roughly 88–89% of its crude oil. Of that, 45% transited the Strait of Hormuz before the crisis — alongside 90% of its LPG imports, over 50% of its LNG, and roughly 40% of its fertilizer. India's crude oil basket has surged from approximately $63 to $146 per barrel in a matter of weeks. Goldman Sachs has cut India's 2026 GDP growth forecast to 5.9%. The rupee has fallen to record lows, hitting ₹94.7/dollar on Friday. PM Modi called the strait's closure "unacceptable" in Parliament. India's response has been quietly bilateral rather than confrontational — no US-led coalition, no naming Iran publicly, no discussion of naval deployment. Instead: two phone calls between Modi and Iran's President Pezeshkian, and tangible results. In mid-March, two Indian-flagged LPG carriers owned by the Shipping Corporation of India were granted safe passage — with the Iranian Navy actively guiding one through, staying in radio contact, verifying the ship's flag, crew nationality, and destination before escorting it along a pre-approved route. Two more followed on March 23. External Affairs Minister Jaishankar's position: there is no "blanket arrangement" — "every ship movement is an individual happening." Twenty-two Indian-flagged vessels remain stuck in the Gulf. India is navigating this crisis through mutual dependence: Iran needs India to keep buying its oil, India needs Iran to keep the strait passable. Source: Daily Brief (Zerodha).

India · Infrastructure
Also in the Daily Brief: India's ₹4,000 crore bet on dredging — why the country that wants bigger ports has to first fight an endless war with silt
The second story in today's Daily Brief is a fascinating infrastructure piece on dredging — the unglamorous but essential business of clearing sediment from harbour floors. The Dredging Corporation of India (DCI) is undertaking a ₹4,000 crore fleet overhaul with 11 new dredgers. India's own guidelines estimate 3 billion cubic metres of dredging is needed over the next decade. The Kolkata port story is the most dramatic: the Hooghly river deposits enormous quantities of Ganga silt into the shipping channel without pause, forcing ₹300–500 crore annually in dredging just to keep the port open. Ships heading to Haldia (100 km downstream) sometimes have to partially unload at neighbouring ports first because the river is too shallow for fully loaded vessels. On the other coast, Cochin silts heavily every monsoon. And at Vizhinjam, Kerala — site of the new international seaport — port construction has disrupted coastal sand movement, eroding fishing villages to the north. Fishers are divided: some demand the port stop dredging; others demand more dredging, because the harbour mouth is silting up and capsizing their boats. The cost problem: soft soil dredging costs ₹110/cubic metre, rock costs ₹4,000/cubic metre — a 36-fold gap that has blown up budgets when soil surveys turn out wrong. Source: Daily Brief (Zerodha).

Global Macro · Japan
Japan's 10-year bond yield hit 2.37% — its highest since 1999. Oil-driven inflation is forcing the Bank of Japan's hand in a way two decades of deflation never could.
Japan's 10-year government bond yield rising to 2.37% is a significant macro signal that extends well beyond Japan's borders. For more than two decades, Japan was the world's leading example of near-zero rates and inflation that refused to appear. The Bank of Japan ran negative rates as recently as early 2024. The Iran war's energy shock has changed the calculus sharply: Japan imports nearly all of its energy (it has almost no domestic fossil fuel production), so the crude oil spike translates directly into inflation. A weakening yen (the dollar has strengthened as a safe haven) amplifies the imported inflation further. Higher bond yields in Japan have historically triggered global ripples — Japanese institutional investors are among the world's largest holders of foreign bonds, and when Japanese yields rise, the relative attractiveness of holding US Treasuries or Indian government bonds diminishes, potentially triggering capital repatriation.
