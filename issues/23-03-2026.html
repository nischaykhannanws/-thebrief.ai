Nifty crashed to 22,512 — then Trump blinked, and everything changed after hours

Lead · Aftermarket Report · Monday

The session was brutal. 290-point gap-down. 3,008 of 3,420 stocks fell. No sector closed green. Then at 6 PM, Trump posted in all caps on Truth Social — and Gift Nifty surged.

You see, today's market had two completely separate acts. Act One: Nifty opened with a massive 290-point gap-down at 22,824, tracking fears that Trump would follow through on his 48-hour ultimatum to bomb Iranian power plants if the Strait of Hormuz wasn't reopened. The index drifted lower through the morning, testing 22,480 by noon. Not only this — Consumer Durables fell 5.17%, the worst of any sector. Only four Nifty 50 stocks ended positive (HCL Tech, Power Grid, Infosys, ONGC). The selling was broad and indiscriminate. PM Modi addressed the Lok Sabha on the conflict — a brief bounce toward 22,650 — but it faded. Nifty settled at 22,512 (−2.60%), Sensex at 72,696 (−2.46%).

Act Two happened after market hours. Trump posted — in capital letters — that the US and Iran had held "very good and productive conversations" and he was instructing the Department of War to postpone all strikes on Iranian power plants and energy infrastructure for five days. "They called, I didn't call. They want to make a deal." Oil fell sharply (WTI dropped to $85, Brent to $91.5 before recovering). Gift Nifty surged. US stock futures rose. The five-day window now defines everything — if talks hold, Tuesday's Nifty expiry could open very differently from how today closed.

RBI Emergency Action
Banking liquidity entered deficit of ₹65,900 crore for first time in 2026 — RBI injects ₹79,256 crore overnight
India's banking system had been running a comfortable ₹2.5 lakh crore surplus between February and mid-March. Today it flipped into a ₹65,900 crore deficit — driven by heavy advance tax outflows and RBI's own currency market interventions to defend the rupee. The RBI responded with an overnight variable rate repo auction injecting ₹79,256 crore to ease tightening cash conditions. Due to this, short-term borrowing costs had been spiking intraday, which the injection is designed to cap.

Lead · ETAuto · Monday

JSW MG Motor India's Windsor EV is selling 70% of its volume in non-metro cities — a data point that rewrites the EV adoption narrative

You see, the dominant assumption in Indian EV market analysis has been that electric vehicles are a metro phenomenon — Bengaluru, Delhi, Mumbai early adopters driving the numbers. The Windsor EV's sales data says something different. JSW MG Motor India reports that 70% of Windsor EV sales are coming from non-metro cities. Not only does this upend the metro-first narrative — it also signals that range anxiety may be less of a barrier than assumed when the right price point and product are in place. The Windsor's battery subscription model, which separates the battery cost from the vehicle cost, appears to be the key unlock for non-metro buyers who can't absorb a high upfront price.

India's chip-design paradox, GLP-1 generics flooding in, CAFE 3 in disarray

5 stories
Lead · The Ken · Monday · by Priyal Mahtta

India wants to be a chip-design hub. Its Design-Linked Incentive scheme is blocking the exact founders who could build it.

You see, India is about to announce Semiconductor Mission 2.0 with an additional ~$10 billion in subsidies. The DLI (Design-Linked Incentive) scheme — which was supposed to unlock India's chip-design potential — has a structural paradox at its core. On one side, the very founders it wants to attract are ineligible: engineers with 20–30 years of experience in Silicon Valley fabs, deep networks, and access to foreign capital are being shut out by an "Indian ownership" clause that requires majority Indian control. On the other side, companies backed by foreign venture capital are excluded entirely before they even begin.

Not only this — the DLI is the opposite of what the PLI (Production-Linked Incentive) scheme did for electronics. PLI attracted Samsung, Foxconn, and Tata by being broadly accessible. DLI is deterring the most capable participants. Put simply, India is trying to build a chip-design ecosystem by filtering out the people most qualified to design chips. The Ken's Priyal Mahtta spoke to founders and investors who want to design and tape-out chips from India but are left wringing their hands. This is a must-read for anyone following India's semiconductor ambitions.

El Niño deep dive, the metaverse's reality check, and the energy shock's worst-case floor

3 reads
Deep Read · The Daily Brief · Zerodha

El Niño's full economic anatomy — from India's power grid and food inflation to Chilean copper, Indonesian nickel, and Argentine soybeans

NOAA issued its El Niño Watch this month — a formal signal that conditions are becoming favourable. By August, there's a 62% probability of El Niño conditions underway. Zerodha's Daily Brief published the year's best single-source explainer on what this means for India. You see, El Niño isn't just a monsoon story. It operates through multiple economic channels simultaneously.

On the domestic side: India's peak power demand was 246 GW in 2024 — the government projects 270 GW this summer. El Niño suppresses the monsoon, draining hydroelectric reservoirs (hydro generation fell 8% in H1 2024 during the last El Niño). Coal steps in. Gas-fired output jumped 50%. But solar gets a boost — fewer monsoon clouds over Gujarat and Rajasthan means more solar irradiance. On the global commodity side: drought in Indonesia raises nickel prices (India imports nearly all its nickel). Floods in Chile's Atacama shut copper mines (India imports copper for cables and transformers). Argentina's bumper soybean harvest is the one India-positive effect. The full piece is on The Daily Brief — essential reading before summer.

Bloomberg · CNN · Axios · Al Jazeera · Euronews

The Iran war's most important day yet — a 5-day diplomatic window opens

5 items
Context: The War So Far

The US-Israel-Iran war started in late February 2026, with US and Israeli strikes on Iran including reportedly destroying nuclear facilities. Iran responded by closing the Strait of Hormuz — through which ~20% of global oil and gas normally passes. This triggered the oil crisis, the rupee's all-time lows, India's oil import crunch (−23% in March), and today's market crash. On Saturday, Trump gave Iran a 48-hour ultimatum: reopen Hormuz or face US strikes on Iranian power plants. The deadline was due Monday evening. Instead, Trump announced the five-day pause.

Breaking · Multiple Sources · Monday 23 March 2026

Trump pauses strikes, says "major points of agreement" reached — Iran denies talks, mediators confirm progress, five-day window now the world's most watched clock

Trump posted on Truth Social that he's instructing the Department of War to pause all strikes on Iranian power plants and energy infrastructure for five days. His stated reason: "very good and productive conversations" with Iran over the past two days, with "major points of agreement" reached. The chief goals remain no nuclear weapons and ending hostilities. He said "they called, I didn't call" — claiming Iran initiated contact. US negotiators reportedly include Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner.

Iran's foreign ministry officially denied any talks were held, saying the claims were inaccurate. Trump explained the denial as "miscommunication within Iranian leadership" — reflecting how fractured Tehran's chain of command has become after so many senior figures were killed in strikes. A US source told Axios that Turkey, Egypt, and Pakistan have been passing messages between the US and Iran, with the foreign ministers of all three holding separate talks with both Witkoff and Iranian FM Araghchi. Egypt's foreign ministry publicly welcomed the announcement and called for "seizing this opportunity." China's diplomat at the scene quoted a Chinese saying: "To untie a belt, the person who tied it is needed." Iranian state television reacted with a graphic that said: "US president backs down following Iran's firm warning." Both sides are claiming the pause as their own victory — which is usually how ceasefires begin.

What's at Stake
If the Strait of Hormuz reopens: oil falls sharply, rupee recovers, India's inflation pressure eases significantly
Trump told CNN on Monday that the Strait of Hormuz will be "open very soon." If that happens, the chain reaction for India is: crude oil falls below $80 → India's import bill shrinks → rupee recovers from ₹94 → RBI has room to ease monetary policy → bond yields fall from 6.82% → domestic inflation pressure eases. Not only this — FII flows could reverse as India becomes more attractive in a lower-oil environment. The five-day window is effectively a countdown to knowing which scenario plays out. Source: CNN · Bloomberg · Aftermarket Report.

Iran's Counter-Threat
Before the pause: Iran had threatened to destroy every power plant and desalination facility across the Gulf if its power grid was hit
Before Trump's announcement, Iran had issued its own counter-threat: if US strikes hit Iranian power plants, Iran would destroy all power plants across West Asia — including those in Gulf Cooperation Council countries. The threat was existential for Gulf states because 90% of GCC drinking water comes from desalination plants that are co-located with power stations and cannot operate without electricity. Due to this, Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Qatar were in the most direct line of fire — and had been putting pressure on all sides to de-escalate. This is why Turkey, Egypt, Pakistan, and Gulf states were all mediating simultaneously. Source: Euronews · Newsweek.
