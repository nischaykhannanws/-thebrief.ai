Lead · Aftermarket Report · Tuesday 7 Apr

Gap-down open at 22,839 on weak global cues — and by the close, Nifty was near session highs at 23,123. 10 of 12 sectors green. IT led, PSU Bank was the lone notable drag.

You see, today was a textbook reversal session. The index gapped down to 22,839 when the opening bell rang — weak cues from global markets, nerves about Brent at $109, and the RBI policy uncertainty all conspiring to pull futures lower overnight. But steady buying interest through the day pushed the index right through those early losses. By the time the session was done, Nifty sat at 23,123.65, near the day's highs, with 10 of 12 sectors in green territory.

Not only this — options positioning for the 13 April expiry tells an interesting story underneath the headline number. Max Call OI sits at 23,500 (resistance zone: 23,300–23,400), and Max Put OI at 23,000 (support zone: 22,800–22,900). Due to this, the market is pricing a consolidation range rather than a breakout — players are not yet convinced this recovery has legs beyond the next 300–400 points. The bigger variable remains external: RBI policy tomorrow and any movement on the US-Iran deadline on the Strait of Hormuz.

Consumer · QSR
Jubilant FoodWorks fell 10%+ to March 2024 lows — same-store sales growth collapsed to 0.2% vs 5% prior quarter
Domino's India operator Jubilant FoodWorks saw one of the sharpest single-day falls in its recent history, touching March 2024 lows. The trigger: same-store sales growth (SSSG) in the latest quarter came in at a barely-there 0.2% — compared to 5% in the preceding quarter. At 0.2%, they are essentially flat. The combination of consumer slowdown, urban discretionary spending fatigue, and elevated food inflation is showing up starkly in the numbers. Not only this — Jubilant's result comes alongside Kalyan Jewellers reporting +64% YoY revenue in Q4 FY26, which suggests that discretionary spending hasn't vanished — it has moved up-market, away from mass QSR.

Breaking · Iran War Day 38 · Tuesday 7 Apr

Trump's Tuesday night deadline: "Every bridge in Iran will be decimated by 12 o'clock tomorrow night." Iran sent a 10-point counter-proposal. Trump called it "significant" but "not good enough." Brent crossed $110. The clock is ticking.

You see, today is the deadline day. President Trump set an 8 PM (EDT) Tuesday deadline for Iran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz or face what he described as the destruction of every bridge and power plant in Iran. Iran's central military command warned of "much more devastating" retaliation if civilian targets are hit. Iranian officials called on citizens to form human chains around power plants.

Not only this — there is a diplomatic thread still alive. Iran sent a 10-point response to US proposals via Pakistani mediators after two weeks of internal deliberation. The response calls for a permanent end to the war (not a ceasefire), an end to Israeli strikes on Lebanon, a protocol for safe passage through the Strait, the lifting of all sanctions, and reconstruction commitments. A US official who saw the response called it "maximalist." Trump acknowledged it was "significant" but said it was "not good enough," keeping the Tuesday deadline in place. Due to this, the outcome of the next 12 hours is the single most consequential variable for Brent crude, the rupee, and global risk appetite — all of which India's RBI must factor into its policy statement tomorrow.

Private credit's $1.8T problem, PSU Ratna reform stuck in committee, GST's broken self-policing, and Adani's think tank in Delhi

4 reads
Lead · Finshots · Finance Deep Dive

Private credit has grown 5x since 2008 to $1.8 trillion globally — and right now, Blue Owl Capital capped withdrawals when investors tried to pull out 20% of a $36B fund. The SaaSpocalypse is the trigger. The cracks were already there.

You see, private credit sounds like a niche institutional concept — but $1.8 trillion is not niche. This is money lent by non-bank asset managers like Blue Owl, BlackRock, Ares, Apollo, and KKR directly to companies, bypassing the traditional banking system. It grew explosively after 2008 because banks pulled back from risky lending, and institutional investors (pension funds, insurers, endowments) needed higher yields than public bonds offered. Well, it worked — until it didn't.

Here is the problem now. About 20% of all private credit — roughly $500 billion — has been lent to mid-sized SaaS companies. Agentic AI has sparked fears that software firms' revenues could collapse as AI agents replace human workers who use those SaaS products. Morgan Stanley has warned that default rates in private credit could rise to 8%, compared to the usual 2–2.5%. Not only this — these are long-duration loans; fund managers cannot demand repayment overnight. When investors tried to pull 20%+ of funds from Blue Owl's $36B private credit pool, the firm capped withdrawals at 5%. Multiple other firms are doing the same.

Due to this, a secondary market discount problem is emerging: buyers in the thin secondary market for private credit are demanding heavy haircuts. And the systemic risk question is pointed at insurers — particularly where PE giants like Apollo and KKR own the very insurers that are financing their own LBO deals. The comparisons to 2008 may be overblown (private credit is under 5% of US GDP versus real estate's 100%+) — but the pre-existing cracks that the SaaSpocalypse has widened were visible as early as September 2025, when Tricolor (subprime auto) and First Brands Group (auto parts) both went bust. A crisis may be looming. Its scale remains unknown.

Zerodha · Policy
India's PSU Ratna system can amplify winners but cannot fix structural failures — and no PSU has ever been downgraded
Zerodha's Daily Brief dissects India's PSU Ratna classification — the system that determines how much investment and operational autonomy a government company gets without ministerial sign-off. The tiers run from Miniratna (Cat I and II) through Navratna to Maharatna. But the system has a structural flaw no one wants to fix: MTNL, which reported a net loss of over ₹3,300 crore in FY24, is still classified as a Navratna. Not a single PSU has ever been downgraded. Meanwhile, 441 of 750 independent director positions on PSU boards are vacant as of October 2024 — meaning PSU boards are effectively ungoverned. The Somanathan Committee reviewed the framework, and the Union Budget FY27 came and went with no announcement. Full detail in the Daily Brief.

Zerodha · Tax Policy
GST's brilliant self-enforcement mechanism has been broken by three design choices — and Indian manufacturers are paying the hidden cost
The second Zerodha Daily Brief piece is a forensic look at why GST — despite its elegant self-policing design — effectively taxes production in India rather than just consumption. The core genius: buyers demand proper invoices to claim input tax credits, which polices sellers. Three design failures broke this mechanism. First, a blocked credits list that denies credits on genuine business inputs like motor vehicles, health insurance, and warehouse rents. Second, narrow refund rules that exclude capital goods and services from refund entitlements. Third, procedural conditions that deny your input credit if your supplier defaults on GST filings — even if you have a valid invoice. Due to this, hidden taxes get embedded into every stage of India's supply chain, making domestic manufacturers less competitive than importers (who pay clean GST) and less competitive as exporters. China fixed its equivalent system between 2009–2016 by opening up credits fully. India has the digital infrastructure to do the same. It lacks the political will.

FY26 auto retail: record 2.97 crore units. Tata Sierra triples, MG EV surges, and VinFast bets big on India's MPV gap

6 stories
Lead · ETAuto · FY26 Auto Retail

India's auto retail closed FY26 at 2.97 crore units — a record. Two-wheelers hit 2.14 crore (+13.4%), passenger vehicles rose 13% to 47.1 lakh. March alone was the best-ever monthly sales month per FADA.

You see, FY26 turned out to be a year of records for Indian auto retail despite everything working against it — elevated fuel prices in the second half, West Asia uncertainty, and a slowing urban consumer. Two-wheelers drove the volume story: 2.14 crore units, up 13.4%, as rural income revival (driven by a good monsoon and rising crop prices) powered mass-market motorcycle demand. Passenger vehicles were equally strong, reaching 47.1 lakh units, up 13% — a number that tells you urban India's appetite for cars is structurally intact even as wallet stress shows up in QSR SSSG.

Not only this — March 2026 itself was the best-ever single month for auto retail in history per FADA data. End-of-year buying, channel destocking incentives, and pre-price-hike urgency all combined to deliver that peak. Due to this, the FY27 base is now a challenging one to beat — especially if fuel costs remain elevated and the RBI holds rates tomorrow without a meaningful cut signal. FADA also flagged that West Asia turmoil could disrupt auto supply chains and raise component costs in the months ahead.
