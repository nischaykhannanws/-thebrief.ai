Aftermarket Report · Groww Digest · Wednesday 25 Mar 2026

Nifty holds the rally at 23,306 — oil drops on ceasefire hopes, India pivots to Russian crude, and the NHAI InvIT debut is a signal

Lead · Aftermarket Report · Wednesday

Nifty opened with a 152-point gap-up, tested 23,450 by noon, then drifted back to close at 23,306 (+1.72%). This was day two of a sustained recovery — and the macro story underneath is moving fast.

You see, today's session was a textbook relief rally anatomy — gap-up open on geopolitical de-escalation hopes, steady buying through the morning, intraday peak around 12:30 PM, then late profit-booking that pulled it ~150 points off the high. All 12 sectors closed green. Consumer Durables led the charge (+3.51%), IT was the lonely laggard at just +0.08% — one of the few sectors still pricing in global uncertainty rather than de-escalation hope. 2,425 stocks advanced vs 852 declined. The broader breadth is holding.

But the bigger story today is not the Nifty level — it's the machinery underneath. Brent crude fell ~4% to ~$94 on ceasefire talk hopes (the US formally handed a 15-point peace plan to Iran via Pakistan). Indian refiners have simultaneously secured 60 million barrels of Russian crude for next month at premiums of $5–$15 over Brent — double the February volumes. S&P Global upgraded India's FY27 growth forecast by 40bps to 7.1%. And the NHAI Raajmarg InvIT listed today, subscribed 14x. These are not coincidental data points — they form a coherent picture: India is actively building supply chain resilience, attracting capital, and being re-rated upward precisely because its policy response to the Iran shock has been faster and more structured than most peers.

Energy · Supply Chain
India secures 60 million barrels of Russian oil for April — double February volumes — at $5–15 premium over Brent
This is the most concrete sign yet that India has found its Iran supply replacement route. Indian refiners have locked in approximately 60 million barrels of Russian crude for delivery next month, according to Kpler data cited in the Aftermarket Report. Volumes more than doubled compared to February. The cargoes were bought at a $5–$15 premium over Brent — so India is paying more per barrel than it would in normal times, but the supply certainty is worth the premium. Not only this — this pivot to Russia is happening even as Iran has reportedly offered its own oil to India at a premium to Brent, signalling Tehran's own desperate need for export revenue. India is managing this crisis with more commodity market sophistication than it gets credit for.

S&P Global upgraded its India FY27 GDP growth forecast to 7.1% (+40bps), citing resilient domestic consumption, improving private investment momentum, and steady export performance. Lead · ETAuto · Wednesday

Honda has reaffirmed India as its global 2-wheeler hub — and across Asia, EV showrooms are seeing an unusual surge as the Iran oil shock makes ICE running costs feel suddenly very real to consumers

Not only this — the Finance Ministry is being approached to extend EV subsidies for 2W and 3Ws, with ~1 million e-2Ws sold against a 1.4 million target — the shortfall suggesting the subsidy itself needs to be more generous, not just longer-running.

The Jaypee collapse, MasterCard's stablecoin bet, Microsoft vs OpenAI, and the cost of Reliance's broken KG-D6 promises

5 reads
Lead · The Daily Brief · Zerodha · Wednesday

The Jaypee Group story is the best piece of long-form financial journalism published this week. From ₹10,000 starting capital and 27% of India's hydropower capacity to ₹75,274 crore in debt, ₹21.5 crore in daily interest, and 6.45 lakh retail shareholders getting zero — in two sections that read like a thriller.

You see, every corporate collapse has a moment where the numbers stopped making sense and the promoters kept going anyway. For Jaypee Group, that moment was FY2014 — the year consolidated revenues hit ₹19,832 crore (impressive) while consolidated borrowings hit ₹72,599 crore (catastrophic). Interest costs that year alone were ₹6,233 crore. The operating profit couldn't cover the interest bill. And yet the group continued expanding.

The Daily Brief's piece on the Jaypee collapse is worth reading in full not just as a post-mortem but as a framework for how not to run a conglomerate during an infrastructure boom. Jaiprakash Gaur started in 1958 with ₹10,000 and built India's premier hydropower constructor — 13 projects across six states and Bhutan, 27% of installed hydro capacity. Then he made the classic conglomerate mistake: cement, then real estate, then the Yamuna Expressway, then hotels, then F1. All simultaneously. All funded by debt. All in a sector where construction timelines are measured in years and interest clocks are measured in days.

The denouement: after selling assets worth ₹36,000 crore over a decade, creditors still had claims of ₹57,185 crore at insolvency. Adani won the bid (₹14,535 crore, NCLT approved). Vedanta is fighting at NCLAT. Lenders take a 73% haircut. And 6.45 lakh shareholders — disproportionately retail — get exactly zero. The remaining prize: land sitting around the Noida International Airport. Read the full Daily Brief piece. It is the best thing in your inbox this week.

Daily Brief · Fintech
MasterCard is paying $1.8 billion for BVNK — a stablecoin infrastructure company. The best explanation of why card networks aren't threatened by crypto, they're adopting it.
The Daily Brief's second piece today is the clearest single explainer of the stablecoin-vs-card-network dynamic available. The key insight: stablecoins changed the settlement layer of payments but not the trust layer. Card networks own the trust layer — merchant acceptance, fraud protection, credit facilities, rewards ecosystems, and dispute resolution. So the future isn't cards OR stablecoins — it's cards with stablecoins underneath (banks settling in USDC instead of through interbank clearing), stablecoins with cards on top (crypto wallet holder spends at merchant via MasterCard-branded card), and in narrow cases, stablecoins without cards at all (B2B cross-border payments). MasterCard's ₹1.8B BVNK acquisition is about owning the infrastructure that makes all three models work. For India: UPI has solved domestic payments — stablecoins add nothing there. But India is the world's largest remittance recipient and those corridors still cost 4–6%. That's the real opportunity. Full piece on The Daily Brief.

Iran War Day 26 · Multiple sources · Wednesday 25 March

The 15-point ceasefire plan, Iran's 5-point counter, a Kuwait airport fire, and possible talks in Islamabad by Friday

Day 26
Breaking · Iran War Day 26 · Wednesday 25 March 2026

The US formally delivered a 15-point ceasefire plan to Iran via Pakistan. Iran rejected it and issued a 5-point counter. Both sides rejected the other's proposal. But possible in-person talks in Islamabad as soon as Friday are being floated by mediators — and Trump has given Iran 4 more days before targeting energy infrastructure.

This is the most structurally significant diplomatic development since the war began on February 28. The US has now formally committed a comprehensive written proposal to paper and delivered it through official mediators (Pakistan). The 15-point plan reportedly covers: sanctions relief, rollback of Iran's nuclear program, limits on missiles, and reopening the Strait of Hormuz.

Iran's 5-point counter is revealing in what it demands: a complete halt to "aggression and assassinations," concrete mechanisms to prevent future attacks, ending the war across all fronts (including Lebanon and Gaza proxy dimensions), Iranian sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz (which may be a non-starter for the US), and war-related reparations. A top US official called the counter "ridiculous and unrealistic." A senior Iranian official said the US was "in no position to negotiate." And yet — mediators from Egypt and Pakistan are pushing for in-person talks in Islamabad as soon as Friday.

Meanwhile the war continues: Iranian drones hit Kuwait International Airport's fuel storage, starting a large fire. Saudi Arabia intercepted drones in its eastern region. Iran fired more missile waves at Israel. Israel continues strikes inside Iran and Lebanon. Trump has granted temporary immunity to Iran's Foreign Minister and Parliament Speaker for the duration of the 5-day negotiation window. The 82nd Airborne is deploying to the region. And 59% of Americans now say US military action has gone too far, per AP-NORC poll — Trump's biggest political liability is not the war itself but the fuel costs it's imposing on red-state Americans.

Finshots · AI
Microsoft vs OpenAI: the "stateful vs stateless" API war that could redefine who controls AI distribution — and Azure's moat
Finshots' piece today is the best summary of the Microsoft-OpenAI legal dispute. The core issue: when Microsoft negotiated its 2025 restructuring deal with OpenAI, it retained a clause that "all stateless API calls" must route through Azure. OpenAI then signed a $50 billion partnership with AWS (Amazon Web Services) for its Frontier enterprise platform. Microsoft says Frontier — even if "stateful" — relies on stateless models underneath, so it still violates the contract. OpenAI and Amazon say Frontier is a product built on top of models, not direct model access. The definitional battle matters because Azure's entire AI valuation premium depends on exclusive OpenAI access. If that exclusivity weakens, Azure becomes "one option among many." What makes this fascinating: the relationship has shifted from asymmetric (OpenAI needed Microsoft's billions and infrastructure in 2019) to near-equal (OpenAI's models are now the most valuable in the industry). A genuinely consequential tech dispute that the Finshots piece explains without jargon. Read it.

Iran War · Diplomacy
The US 15-point plan covers sanctions relief, nuclear rollback, missile limits, Hormuz reopening. Iran's counter demands Hormuz sovereignty and war reparations.
The 15-point US proposal, delivered by Pakistan, was described by Egyptian officials as a "comprehensive deal." Its key pillars: phased sanctions relief tied to nuclear program rollback (including disposal of 460kg of 60% enriched uranium), limits on ballistic missiles, verification by the IAEA, and reopening of the Strait of Hormuz as the central economic demand. Iran's 5-point counter included demands that are structurally incompatible with the US position: Iranian sovereignty over Hormuz (meaning the right to close it), war reparations, and a guarantee that the war will never be "reimposed." The IRGC has consolidated power inside Tehran, making it likely that the most hawkish faction — not the diplomats — is setting the counter-proposal terms. Mediators are pushing for an in-person meeting in Islamabad between Iranian and American representatives by Friday. The 5-day ceasefire window Trump announced on Monday expires Saturday.
