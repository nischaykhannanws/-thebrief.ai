Lead · Aftermarket Report · Tuesday

Nifty opened flat near 24,375, found its footing, and marched to 24,576 — the best close in several sessions. FMCG was the star. VIX hit a five-day low at 17.53. Eleven of twelve sectors finished green.

Well, you see, today was a session that had all the ingredients of genuine conviction — not just a gap-up bounce. Nifty opened near 24,375 on mixed global cues, but the selling pressure was muted from the start. Within the first hour, buyers stepped in, and the index simply marched higher through the session in a clean, steady grind. The final close of 24,576.60 is the best in several sessions, and the breadth confirmed it: 335 advances versus 161 declines in the Nifty 500. Eleven of twelve sectors finished in the green.

Not only this — the single biggest driver was Nestle India. Its Q4FY26 numbers — profit up 27 per cent, revenue up 23 per cent — sent the stock up 7 per cent and lifted the entire FMCG index by 2.55 per cent. When one stock can move an entire sector index by that quantum, you know the market is in a momentum-sensitive state. Due to this, the calm VIX reading (17.53, the lowest in five days) tells the real story: fear has stepped back, and buyers are comfortable holding overnight positions again.

Trump says ceasefire ends Wednesday evening Washington time and it's "highly unlikely" he'd extend it. JD Vance heads to Pakistan for talks. Iran seizes the narrative — and a cargo ship.

Meanwhile, Iran's top negotiator Ghalibaf — a regime insider with deep IRGC roots — is expected to lead the Iranian delegation in Islamabad if Tehran shows up at all. Mixed signals abound: Iranian officials publicly said they would not negotiate in Pakistan; Iranian sources told CNN they would send a delegation. Due to this ambiguity, markets are watching this more closely than any other single variable right now.

India breaks the GLP-1 monopoly (sort of), cabotage gets six more months, and an ex-chip engineer's systematic trading lessons

4 reads
Lead · The Daily Brief · Zerodha · Tuesday

Semaglutide patent expired in India on March 20. Five generic versions launched within days. Price collapsed 90%. But here's why "India breaks the GLP-1 monopoly" is a far more complicated headline than it sounds.

Well, you see, the story sounds triumphant at first telling. Novo Nordisk's semaglutide patent — the molecule behind Ozempic and Wegovy — expired in India. Dr. Reddy's, Mankind, Zydus, Natco, and Glenmark all launched generics within days. The price of a monthly course collapsed from ₹8,800–₹11,175 to ₹1,290. A 90 per cent price drop is extraordinary by any measure. But Zerodha's Daily Brief peels back the headline and what's underneath is considerably more complex.

Semaglutide has nearly 600 atoms that all have to be exactly right — compare that with aspirin's 21. The manufacturing process, called solid-phase peptide synthesis, generates up to 14,000 kg of waste per kilogram of drug produced. Even at state-of-the-art synthesis, practical purity tops out around 57 per cent. Not only this — Health Canada has already asked both Dr. Reddy's and Sandoz for additional data before approval, something that doesn't happen with standard small-molecule drugs. And while Indian firms were building semaglutide capacity, Eli Lilly's tirzepatide (Mounjaro) — which targets two hormone receptors versus semaglutide's one — had already overtaken Wegovy as India's top-selling GLP-1 by value. Due to this, the innovators have simply moved to the next floor. The US patent doesn't expire until 2032. India represents just 0.3 per cent of Novo Nordisk's global GLP-1 revenues.

Finshots · Shipping Policy
India's cabotage relaxation was supposed to end today — the government just extended it by another six months, until October
India eased its cabotage rules back in 2018, allowing foreign-flagged ships to carry domestic cargo on coastal routes without a licence. April 21 was the day it was supposed to end. It didn't. The government extended it six more months, until October — after lobbying from both Indian and foreign shipping lines. Well, you see, the core tension is this: Indian shippers say foreign carriers are predatorily undercutting them; foreign carriers counter they slashed freight rates by up to 80 per cent and benefited exporters. India's fleet grew by 92 vessels in FY26 including 50 coastal ones, and gross tonnage added was nearly three times the year before. But policy clarity is the real problem — eight years of rolling extensions make long-term coastal network investment from foreign carriers risky. Source: Finshots.

Policy · Trade Research
India's Patents Amendment Act 2002 boosted high-tech exports by ~17% — but the gains were concentrated and low-tech firms barely moved
A 2025 IIFT Centre for WTO Studies paper found that high-tech firms exported approximately 17 per cent more than low-tech peers in the post-2002 reform years, and their technology adoption expenditure rose more than threefold. The mechanism: stronger patents made international supply chain partners more willing to bring Indian manufacturers into genuine technology partnerships on better terms. But 62 per cent of firms in the dataset were low-tech — and for them, the reform barely moved the needle. Due to this, the reform amplified existing capability; it didn't create new capability where none existed. Bigger, high-tech firms disproportionately benefited. Source: The Daily Brief (Zerodha).

E85 policy incoming, SML Mahindra enters e-buses, Maruti guides double-digit growth, and India's EV charging gap widens

6 stories
Lead · ETAuto · Tuesday

The government is expected to issue a draft E85 fuel policy in the near term — a blend of 85% ethanol and 15% petrol that could reshape India's entire transport fuel economics

Well, you see, E85 isn't just a fuel story — it's a strategic one. India currently mandates E20 blending (20 per cent ethanol in petrol), and the government has been steadily pushing its ethanol blending targets as a tool to reduce the petroleum import bill and cut transport sector emissions. E85 — 85 per cent ethanol, 15 per cent petrol — would be a step-change in that direction, but it requires significant changes to vehicle engines, fuel infrastructure, and agricultural policy simultaneously.

Not only this — the timing matters. West Asia tensions have made India's dependence on crude oil imports a live national security issue, not just an economic one. An aggressive ethanol blending push reduces the import bill and gives India more control over its transport fuel supply chain. Due to this, the E85 draft policy, when it comes, will be watched not just by automakers recalibrating flex-fuel engine strategies — but by anyone tracking India's energy import dependence thesis. Maruti Suzuki and Bajaj Auto have both been quietly working on flex-fuel platforms in anticipation of exactly this regulatory direction.
