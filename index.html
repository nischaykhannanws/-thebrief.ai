Finshots · Today's Story

What is an industry, anyway? The Supreme Court has been arguing about it for 48 years.

A nine-judge Supreme Court bench began hearing on March 17 — 48 years after the 1978 Bangalore Water Supply v. R. Rajappa ruling that created the "Triple Test" for what counts as an "industry." The answer affects every hospital worker, teacher, and government employee in India. Well, let's find out — the full story is on Finshots.

Read on Finshots
"The 1978 judgment produced a 'docket explosion' — too many cases in labour courts because nobody could agree on what counted as an industry."
— Finshots, summarising the Supreme Court's 2005 observation
The Triple Test (1978):
1. Systematic & organised activity?
2. Employer–employee cooperation?
3. Produces goods/services for human needs?

If yes to all three — it's an "industry." The bench has reserved its judgment.

Two long reads worth your time tonight

The Daily Brief by Zerodha · Today

India has 26,000 licensed commercial pilots. It still can't fly all its planes on time. Here's why.

You see, India doesn't actually have a pilot shortage. What it has is a captain shortage, a simulator shortage, a training pilot shortage, and an examiner shortage. These distinctions matter — a lot. The DGCA issued a record 1,628 Commercial Pilot Licences (CPLs) in 2024. But a CPL is just the beginning of a very long funnel. To command a commercial flight, a pilot legally needs 1,500 hours — airlines in practice demand 2,500–3,500. Getting there requires type ratings (₹25 lakh, scarce simulator slots), airline induction programmes, and a DGCA viva conducted by examiners of which half the sanctioned posts sit vacant.

Not only this — the 2023 FDTL (Flight Duty Time Limitation) reforms, which brought India closer to international standards, required airlines to reduce pilot workloads by 10–25%. Due to this, demand for experienced pilots spiked across the industry simultaneously. IndiGo, which had been running at just 7.6 pilots per aircraft (global norm: 12–16), found itself nearly 700 pilots short almost overnight. Gulf carriers — Emirates, Etihad, Riyadh Air — recruiting India's most experienced captains makes the problem structurally self-reinforcing. India even petitioned ICAO for a global code of conduct on cross-border pilot recruitment. The proposal was rejected.
