Holiday Markets Brief · Thursday 26 March 2026

NSE and BSE are closed today for Ram Navami. The last close was Wednesday's Nifty at 23,306 (+1.72%) — a second straight day of strong gains. Here's the week in context and what to watch when markets reopen on Friday.

You see, the two-day relief rally (Tuesday +1.78% expiry bounce → Wednesday +1.72%) has taken Nifty from the lows of 22,791 back above 23,300. The trigger was straightforward: ceasefire diplomacy hopes + Brent crude falling ~4% on Wednesday created the classic geopolitical relief trade. The Nifty opened with a 152-point gap on Wednesday, climbed to test 23,450 around noon, then drifted back to close at 23,306. All 12 sectors green. Consumer Durables led (+3.51%), IT was the lone laggard (+0.08%). Bank Nifty added 2.10% to close at 53,708. Fin Nifty up 2.35% to 25,056.

But here's the important framing: oil prices climbed back Thursday on fading de-escalation hopes (Iran rejected talks, strikes intensified), which means the rally's macro tailwind is already wobbling. When markets reopen Friday, sentiment will hinge on the Iran news cycle — specifically whether the Pakistan-mediated indirect talks produce anything tangible, or whether Trump follows through on his "unleash hell" threat to target Iranian energy infrastructure. ICICI Direct's latest note is the most important piece of market reading for the weekend — they believe the bulk of the correction is over and a sharp recovery into the April series is "highly probable."

Honda is closing in on Hero Moto's crown, India is building a rare earth supply chain, Exide bets big on EV batteries, and the fuel panic shows why EVs win

8 stories
Lead · ETAuto · Thursday 26 March 2026

Honda Motorcycle & Scooter India has closed the gap with Hero MotoCorp to its narrowest in FY26 — and the question being asked in the industry is not whether Honda will overtake, but when. Meanwhile, the government told the public today to stop panic-buying fuel. Both stories are connected.

You see, the Honda vs Hero 2-wheeler market share story has been building quietly for two years. In the first 11 months of FY26, Hero MotoCorp dispatched 5.5 million units to dealer outlets — Honda followed at 5.2 million. That 300,000 unit gap is the smallest it has ever been in a fiscal year, and FY26 ends in four days. The last time Honda was this close to Hero was never. The conventional wisdom in the Indian 2W industry was that Hero's rural network, sports bike heritage, and price-ladder dominance in entry-level commuters made it untouchable. Honda has been systematically dismantling that advantage — through the Activa's unshakeable hold on the scooter segment, through new premium commuter launches, and now through the global 2W hub strategy that puts India at the centre of Honda's worldwide expansion.

The government's "no fuel shortage, stop panic buying" message today is the other side of this story. When oil companies have to publicly reassure people about petrol and LPG availability — at a time when pump queues are forming and OMCs are issuing advisories in Telangana and other states — the EV total cost of ownership argument writes itself. Not only this — the Ministry of Heavy Industries today explicitly told the auto sector to shift away from oil-based fuel to electricity. The message is coming from the top. The question is only timing and infrastructure.

Critical Minerals · Supply Chain
India plans 5,000 tonne rare earth magnet capacity by 2030 — domestic demand is 4,000 tonnes today, expected to double. A supply chain bet on the EV transition.
Minister Jitendra Singh announced India's plan to build 5,000 tonnes per annum of rare earth magnet manufacturing capacity by 2030. Rare earth magnets (REMs) — specifically neodymium-iron-boron magnets — are essential for EV motors, wind turbines, and industrial motors. India's domestic demand is approximately 4,000 tonnes today and is expected to double by 2030 as EV penetration scales up. Currently, India imports the vast majority of REMs, primarily from China (which controls over 85% of global processing). Building domestic REM capacity is therefore both an EV supply chain imperative and a China-diversification play. Not only this — with the Iran war disrupting rare earth shipping routes through the Gulf, the urgency around domestic mineral processing has increased sharply. Source: ETAuto.

Finshots · The Ken

Can India become the world's pharmacy for weight-loss drugs? And why airlines now charge you for the seat they legally can't charge you for.

2 reads
Lead · Finshots · Thursday 26 March 2026

The semaglutide patent expired in India last weekend. A dozen drugmakers have lined up to launch generics. The GLP-1 opportunity is real and enormous — but making an injectable pen that delivers a weekly dose of a peptide drug is nothing like making a tablet. India's "world pharmacy" crown is genuinely at stake here.

You see, when Finshots says India could become the pharmacy for weight-loss drugs, they're pointing at a once-in-a-generation pharmaceutical opportunity — but they're also honest about why it's harder than it sounds. Novo Nordisk's semaglutide (sold as Wegovy for obesity, Ozempic for diabetes) costs ₹8,800–10,000 per month in India. Generics could price 60% lower. India has 10 crore diabetics and a rapidly growing obesity burden. The domestic market alone is massive. The export opportunity is larger — the patent has also expired in Canada, Turkey, and Brazil this year. Those three countries account for roughly 30% of the world's population.

But here's where the story gets complicated. The dominant delivery form for semaglutide is not a pill — it's an injectable pen. A precision-engineered device with glass cartridges, ultra-fine needles, dose mechanisms, and springs. India has experience with syringes and some insulin pens. But high-end GLP-1 pen components have historically been imported — needles from Japan, glass from Germany, pen mechanisms from Switzerland. Add a cold chain requirement (2–8°C from factory to pharmacy) that is improving but patchy outside metros. And then add China: Beijing's semaglutide patents also expired last week, and Chinese manufacturers have vertically integrated factories that go from peptide synthesis all the way to pen assembly for export. India's price advantage means nothing if China can deliver the full package. The Finshots piece is a must-read for anyone tracking India pharma.

The Ken · Aviation
The Ken: Airlines can no longer charge you for most seats. They will make you pay anyway. One hand giveth, the other taketh away.
The government's attempt to protect airline passengers from seat selection fees is a case study in regulatory arbitrage. The story: regulators banned or capped seat selection fees — airlines could no longer charge passengers for the bulk of standard seats. The intent was clear: protect flyers from being nickeled-and-dimed on the most basic expectation of their ticket, a seat. The outcome: airlines simply reclassified seats. Seats near emergency exits (more legroom) — charge. Window seats — charge. Bulkhead seats — charge. Any seat the airline deems "preferred" — charge. The category of seats you cannot charge for is now a shrinking rump of middle seats in the worst rows. The regulatory rule was technically followed. The revenue stream was preserved. The Ken's piece on this is a sharp piece of consumer-side reporting on how aviation regulation tends to work in India — intent is good, implementation leaves loopholes, airlines exploit them systematically. Iran War Day 27 · NPR · CNN · Al Jazeera · Fox News · CNBC · Thursday 26 March 2026

Day 27: Pakistan confirms indirect US-Iran talks publicly. Iran parliament drafts Hormuz fee law. Strikes intensify. Oil climbs back. Trump: "Unleash hell."

Day 27
Breaking · Iran War Day 27 · Thursday 26 March 2026

Thursday brought the most contradictory Iran day yet: Pakistan publicly acknowledged it is mediating indirect US-Iran talks — the first official confirmation. Iran's parliament began drafting a law to formally charge ships transit fees in the Strait of Hormuz. US-Israeli strikes on Isfahan "increased in number and intensity." Oil prices climbed back up on fading de-escalation hopes. And the White House warned Trump will "unleash hell" if no deal is struck.

The gap between the two sides' public narratives has never been wider. Trump insists Iran "wants to make a deal so badly but is afraid to say it." Iran's Foreign Minister Araghchi says the exchange of messages "does not mean negotiations" and that Tehran will end the war "on our own terms." Pakistan's top diplomat Ishaq Dar publicly confirmed for the first time that indirect talks are taking place "through messages relayed by Pakistan" — with Egypt and Turkey also involved. This was the first Islamabad confirmation of its mediating role.

The Hormuz fee development is the most structurally significant thing to happen today. Iran's parliament is pursuing a plan to formally codify sovereignty over the Strait and charge vessels transit fees. The GCC Secretary-General said Iran's actions have "overcome all red lines." ADNOC's CEO called it "economic terrorism." The US has been desanctioning Russian oil and waiving Jones Act shipping rules to keep oil markets stable — but if Hormuz transit fees become formal Iranian law, the legal complexity of the entire global energy supply chain changes permanently. RAND analyst Raphael Cohen's framing is the clearest: "Either you cut a deal now or face potentially more intense consequences down the road." Sources: NPR / CNN / Al Jazeera / CNBC.

Iran War · Hormuz
Iran's parliament is drafting a law to formalize sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz and charge ships transit fees — if passed, it changes the global shipping legal framework permanently
Iranian state media (Tasnim and Fars) reported that Iran's parliament is pursuing legislation to formally codify Iran's sovereignty and control over the Strait of Hormuz, while creating a revenue mechanism through vessel transit fees. MP Mohammadreza Rezaei Kouchi was cited as saying the plan would "formally codify Iran's sovereignty, control and oversight over the Strait, while also creating a source of revenue." The Strait is considered an international waterway under the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea — Iran's claim to charge transit fees would be a fundamental challenge to that legal framework. The GCC Secretary-General al-Budaiwi said Iran's actions, including attacks and closing Hormuz, have "overcome all red lines." ADNOC's CEO Sultan al-Jaber: "When Iran holds Hormuz hostage, every nation pays the ransom, at the gas pump, at the grocery store and at the pharmacy." Germany said it could help secure the waterway once a ceasefire is agreed. Sources: NPR / Al Jazeera.

Analysis · RAND / CNBC
RAND: Trump is using troop deployments as coercive diplomacy, not invasion prep. "Either cut a deal now or face more intense consequences." A "forever war" scenario is the bear case.
CNBC's analysis piece citing RAND analyst Raphael Cohen is the clearest framing of the US strategic logic: the deployment of the 82nd Airborne and additional Marines is not preparation for a ground invasion of Iran — it's designed to increase leverage at the negotiating table. Trump is essentially running a coercive bargaining play: "Make a deal now, or face an escalation you won't survive." Cohen's bear case: if talks stall and Iran refuses to engage, the US and Israel continue bombing, Iran continues resisting, and the conflict becomes a "forever war" — neither side winning decisively, but global energy markets disrupted indefinitely. The path to de-escalation requires changes in Iran's power structure (the IRGC currently has consolidated power and is setting maximalist terms) or a Trump pivot. Both are possible. Neither is imminent. Brent staying above $90–95 remains the base case for now. Source: CNBC.
