The questions India is still trying to answer

5 stories
Finshots · March 20

The Supreme Court has been arguing about what an "industry" is since 1978. It still isn't settled.

A nine-judge bench began hearing on March 17 — 48 years after the original ruling — on a question that affects every hospital worker, teacher, and government employee in India. Read the full story from Finshots.

Read on Finshots

The 1978 Bangalore Water Supply v. R. Rajappa judgment created the Triple Test for determining what counts as an "industry" under the Industrial Disputes Act, 1947: Was the activity systematic? Did it involve employer-employee cooperation? Did it produce goods or services for human needs?

The test brought hospitals, universities, and government bodies under labour law protection — but also created the right to strike in essential services. Decades of conflicting court judgments followed.

"If Parliament had already moved on with the new Industrial Relations Code, what exactly was the Court being asked to settle?" — Finshots
The Daily Brief by Zerodha · Big Read

India doesn't have a pilot shortage. What it has is a captain shortage, a simulator shortage, a training pilot shortage, and an examiner shortage. The DGCA issued a record 1,628 commercial pilot licences in 2024 — yet India's six major airlines run at only 7.6–9 pilots per aircraft, well below the global norm of 12–16. New FDTL (flight duty time limitation) rules that took effect in late 2025 forced airlines to need 10–25% more pilots overnight. IndiGo, running the thinnest roster, found itself 700 pilots short almost immediately. The funnel has a wide mouth and a very narrow neck.

India's pilot problem is not what you think it is

Full story
The Daily Brief · Pranav & Kashish

India has 26,000 licensed commercial pilots. And it still can't fly all its planes on time.

You tried to book a flight last month and half the morning slots were already cancelled. Airlines are paying pilots 40% salary hikes and losing them to Gulf carriers anyway. But the government says there is no shortage. Well, let's find out how all of these things can be true at the same time.

Understanding the difference between a pilot and a captain

Back in the day, flying in India was a government monopoly. Air India and Indian Airlines set the pace, trained slowly, and hired conservatively. The explosion of private aviation — IndiGo, SpiceJet, Akasa, Air India's privatisation — changed all of that. India's aviation sector went from sleepy to the third-largest in the world by passengers in under two decades.

Not only did passenger numbers explode — so did aircraft orders. India's airlines have currently ordered roughly 1,700 new aircraft, with IndiGo and Air India alone seeking around a thousand between them. The industry needs roughly 2,000 to 2,100 new pilots a year to service those planes.

India issues about 1,600 commercial pilot licences (CPLs) a year. The gap is notable, but as the Zerodha Daily Brief points out — the mouth of the funnel was never the problem.

Also read: Whither India's Pilots? — The full story on The Daily Brief

Understanding the funnel

Here is how it actually works. A Commercial Pilot Licence (CPL) requires 1.5–2 years of training, 200 flying hours, and over half a crore in fees. That gets you the CPL. It does not get you into a cockpit.

To fly an Airbus A320 — the aircraft that makes up 90 per cent of India's fleet — you need a type rating: another 2 months of training, another ₹25 lakh, and a slot on a Level D flight simulator. India has very few of these, concentrated in Bengaluru, Gurgaon, and Hyderabad.

To become a captain — the pilot-in-command on a commercial flight — you legally need 1,500 hours. Airlines require 2,500–3,500 in practice. Then a DGCA viva examination. And there is a shortage of DGCA examiners. Half the technical posts at the regulator are currently vacant.

Put simply: a CPL holder is to a commercial airline captain what someone with a driving licence is to an F1 driver. The licence is just the beginning.

What the new FDTL rules broke — and what they revealed

In 2023, the Directorate General of Civil Aviation (DGCA) published revised Flight Duty Time Limitations (FDTL) — the rules governing how many hours a pilot can fly, how long they can be on duty, and how much rest they need. The new norms brought India closer to international standards: 48 hours of weekly rest (up from 36), night landing limits tightened from six to two per week, standby time counted against flyable hours.

Due to this, airlines needed 10–25 per cent more pilots overnight to fly the same schedule. Since this was happening across the whole industry simultaneously, demand for experienced pilots leapt up all at once.

"Between 2022 and 2024, IndiGo hired 14 pilots for every plane acquired. Air India hired 23. When the FDTL norms kicked in, IndiGo found itself almost 700 pilots short." — The Daily Brief, Zerodha

Are India's pilots here to stay?

That said, what makes the situation genuinely hard to fix is the Gulf carrier problem. Emirates, Etihad, and Riyadh Air have been actively recruiting Indian pilots — specifically captains, who are the most experienced and the hardest to replace. India formally petitioned ICAO for a global code of conduct to limit cross-border pilot poaching. The proposal was rejected.

In addition to this, the Gulf war is temporarily reducing the pull — fewer Gulf flights means less Gulf recruitment — but as the Zerodha Daily Brief notes, once it recovers, the pull will begin again.

This goes to show that India does not have a pilot shortage. What it has is a captain shortage, a simulator shortage, a training pilot shortage, and an examiner shortage. Unfortunately, all four of those are exactly what actually matter for getting a plane in the air.
