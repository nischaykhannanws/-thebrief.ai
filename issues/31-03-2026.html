FY27 Market Outlook · Tuesday 31 March 2026

FY26 ended at Nifty 22,331 on Monday — a year that opened above 22,000, hit a high near 26,300, and closed almost flat in rupee terms. In dollar terms, it was a loss year: the rupee fell ~14% over FY26. Markets reopen on April 2 into a loaded first week — the April 6 Iran deadline is the single biggest binary event for Indian equities since the war began.

Well, you see, the setup for FY27's first week is unusually binary. Two scenarios dominate everything else. In scenario one, the April 6 deadline passes without new escalation — or better, Iran makes a credible commitment on Hormuz. WTI would fall sharply, possibly $20–30/barrel in days. The rupee would recover from its ₹95.22 record low. Bond yields would ease back from 7%. The Nifty could rally 5–8% in the first week of FY27. In scenario two, US strikes on Iranian energy infrastructure happen. WTI spikes toward $120–130. The rupee could test ₹100. Bond yields push higher. RBI faces a painful choice between currency defence and growth support. Nifty tests 21,000. Not only this — the options market is pricing in an unusually wide implied volatility range for April, which tells you that institutional money is not taking a confident directional view. They are hedging both scenarios.

Beyond the Iran binary, four regulatory frameworks switch on from April 1 — all simultaneously: SEBI's algo trading rules (static IPs mandatory, market orders need protection parameters), RBI's NOP-INR cap on banks ($100mn net open forex positions — this already triggered Bank Nifty -4% last Monday), RBI's new gold-loan NBFC guidelines (Manappuram, Muthoot facing margin compression), and the new financial year accounting cycle beginning (Q4 FY26 earnings start in April — the first quarter to fully capture the Iran war's P&L impact). The OI setup from Monday: Max Call OI at 22,500 (resistance 22,700–22,800), Max Put OI at 22,500/22,000 (support 22,100–22,000). The April 7 expiry will be the first major test of where smart money is positioned post-holiday.

Regulation · Gold · PE
Bain Capital spent ₹4,385 crore on India's gold-loan sector — and the RBI's new rules go live tomorrow, compressing the exact margins Bain paid for
Bain Capital is acquiring a controlling stake in Manappuram Finance — India's second-largest gold-loan NBFC — for ₹4,385 crore ($463mn), the largest-ever PE bet in the sector. The timing is striking: new RBI guidelines for gold-loan NBFCs take effect April 1, tightening loan-to-value caps, restricting bullet repayment products, and enforcing stricter auction norms. The business model Bain is buying will look different on April 2 than it did when the deal was struck. That said, the structural thesis remains intact over a 5–7 year holding period: India holds over 25,000 tonnes of household gold (the world's largest private stockpile), and formalising access to that collateral is a generational opportunity. Gold prices near all-time highs ($4,600/oz) also mean the loan book's collateral cushion is at its widest. The near-term question is whether the regulatory compression squeezes margins faster than geographic expansion can offset it. Source: The Ken.

ETAuto · Tuesday 31 March 2026

FY26 was Indian auto's best year on record — despite a war, an oil shock, and a currency in freefall. Tractor companies are reading migration signals. Renault brought the Duster back. And Ather just drew a floor.

3 stories
Lead · ETAuto · Tuesday 31 March 2026

India's auto industry is closing FY26 at all-time highs across multiple segments — a remarkable result given that the last five weeks of the financial year played out under an active oil war, a currency at record lows, and an LPG shortage. The question for FY27 is whether the structural drivers that powered FY26 — premiumisation, EV adoption, rural demand — can continue to outrun the macro headwinds.

You see, the headline FY26 auto numbers look contradictory at first. Passenger vehicles hit all-time annual sales. Electric 2-wheelers hit a record high in March alone. Commercial vehicle volumes held firm despite higher diesel costs. And this happened while the rupee fell 14%, LPG became scarce for commercial users, and fuel uncertainty made every purchase decision harder. The explanation is layered: India's vehicle penetration remains low by global standards, so there is structural pent-up demand that a supply disruption can temporarily slow but not destroy. Premiumisation — buyers upgrading from entry-level to mid-segment — continued because it was driven by income growth and aspirational demand, not fuel prices. And EV adoption in the 2-wheeler segment actually accelerated because the war made the total cost of ownership argument for EVs dramatically clearer. Back in the day, "how much money will I save on petrol" was a five-year calculation. Now it is a three-month one.

For FY27, three ETAuto stories from today frame what to watch. Tractor companies are tracking urban-to-rural migration as an early demand signal — if migrant workers coming back from the Gulf and from urban construction sites bring savings home, kharif season equipment buying could be strong. Renault unveiled the new Duster in Chennai — the return of a nameplate that created India's affordable SUV segment, now repositioned for a fuel-volatile market. And Ather confirmed its next electric scooter won't be priced below ₹1 lakh — a deliberate brand line in a segment filling up with budget competition. Source: ETAuto.

Rural Economy · Tractors
Tractor companies betting on migrant worker return — Gulf job losses and urban construction slowdown could push rural demand higher in FY27
Tractors are one of India's most reliable rural economic barometers. When rural households receive an income injection — from good crops, government transfers, or returning migrants with savings — they invest in farm productivity. The Iran war has created a specific migration mechanism: Gulf-dependent households (roughly 30% of India's remittances come from Gulf nations) are seeing income stress as construction projects slow in UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Kuwait. Meanwhile, urban construction in India has slowed as imported materials become expensive. Workers displaced from both these streams are returning to their villages with some savings intact. Not only this — the kharif planting season begins in June, so any migration-driven rural income boost landing in April-May arrives at exactly the moment when pre-season equipment purchases are made. Tractor companies building this variable into Q1 FY27 production forecasts are reading the leading indicator correctly — whether the magnitude of migration is large enough to move the demand needle is the open question. Auto · Renault · SUV
Renault unveiled the new Duster in Chennai — India's most-anticipated automotive return, timed for a fuel-volatile market that suits its diesel heritage
The original Renault Duster launched in India in 2012 and essentially created the affordable SUV segment — before Hyundai Creta, before Kia Seltos, before Tata Nexon made it a battlefield. Renault discontinued it in India in 2022, unable to hold its position against domestic players with deeper dealer networks. The 2026 return is on a completely new platform, unveiled in Chennai rather than Delhi — a pointed nod to the Renault-Nissan Oragadam manufacturing plant where the vehicle will be built. The timing is sharper than it looks: the Iran war's fuel price shock has made buyers more cost-per-kilometre conscious than at any point in the last decade, and the Duster's diesel heritage (known for strong efficiency and highway range) fits this environment. Renault's challenge remains the same as it was in 2022: after-sales service network. Indian buyers forgive a lot, but not an unavailable service centre. Source: ETAuto.

EV · Ather · Brand
Ather's next 2-wheeler won't be priced below ₹1 lakh — premium positioning as budget EVs flood the segment below
Ather Energy's decision to floor its next product at ₹1 lakh is a brand strategy call, not a cost constraint. The company is deliberately staying out of the sub-₹1 lakh EV segment — now crowded with Ola Electric's mass-market models, TVS iQube entry variants, and new entrants competing on hardware price. Put simply, Ather is a technology-forward, software-first brand: its products run on a proprietary OS with OTA updates, advanced ride analytics, and a connected ecosystem. Pricing below ₹1 lakh would require stripping exactly the components that define its identity. The Iran-driven fuel cost surge has structurally improved the TCO case for every EV — meaning buyers who were previously constrained to cheap transportation are ready to spend more. Ather's bet is that the first-upgrade buyer — moving from an old petrol scooter to their first quality EV — will spend ₹1–1.5 lakh rather than chasing the cheapest option. Source: ETAuto.

Finshots · Daily Brief (Zerodha) · The Ken

Meta and YouTube were just held liable for how their platforms are designed — not what users post. The legal shift that could rewrite social media's business model. Plus: India's AI copyright dilemma.

2 reads
Lead · Finshots · Tuesday 31 March 2026

You've been using Instagram for years. The algorithm keeps feeding you more of whatever holds your attention — reels, outrage, beauty standards, the rabbit hole of 3am scrolling. You assumed that was just how the app worked. Last week, two US courts decided that "just how the app works" is exactly what Meta and YouTube should be held legally responsible for. Well, let's find out why that changes everything.

Back in the day, social media platforms had near-perfect legal armour — Section 230 of the US Communications Decency Act. Put simply: platforms are not publishers. They host content; they don't create it. If a user posts something harmful, the platform isn't liable. This protection allowed Facebook, YouTube, and Instagram to scale from startups into trillion-dollar companies without worrying about what their users said. The internet as we know it was built on top of this principle.

Then came two cases that cracked the foundation. New Mexico sued Meta and won a $375 million verdict over Instagram's child safety failures. Then a Los Angeles jury found Meta and YouTube negligent not for specific content, but for their platform design — the algorithms, the infinite scroll, the notification engineering, the features built to maximise time-on-platform. This is the product liability framework. When car manufacturers made cars without seatbelts, they faced liability for that design choice. Tobacco companies faced liability for designing cigarettes to be maximally addictive. The argument now being made in courts: when Meta engineers an algorithm it knows maximises engagement for adolescents at the cost of their mental health, that is a defective product — and the designer bears liability. Not only this — the EU's Digital Services Act, the UK's Online Safety Act, and Brazil's ECA Digital are all moving in this direction. In India, a parliamentary standing committee is already recommending "safety-by-design standards" for social media platforms. Due to this, the question is not whether product liability comes to social media. It is when, and in which jurisdiction it becomes enforceable enough to actually change platform architecture. Source: Finshots / Daily Brief (Zerodha).

The Ken · AI · Policy
India's AI copyright three-body problem — a commerce ministry panel proposes blanket consent for AI training, but three competing interests make any stable solution nearly impossible
India's commerce ministry panel has proposed allowing AI companies blanket consent to use copyrighted material for training, with a royalty mechanism. The Ken's framing calls it a "three-body problem" — named after the physics problem where three gravitational bodies in space resist any stable orbit. The three interests: creators (want compensation and control over how their work is used), AI companies (want broad, cheap access to training data — the scale of data needed makes individual licensing impractical), and the public interest in AI development (needs speed and scale, which is hurt by heavy restrictions). Just as three gravitational bodies can't find a stable equilibrium, these three interests can't all be satisfied simultaneously. Giving creators full control slows AI development. Giving AI companies free access undermines creators' livelihoods. Splitting the difference through royalties creates a massive valuation problem: how do you price a poem in a dataset of 500 billion tokens? Source: The Ken.

The Ken · In The Money (Zerodha) · Tuesday 31 March 2026

Two deep reads for a holiday edition — Bain's ₹4,385 crore gold bet runs into the RBI's rulebook. And SEBI's algo framework, years in the making, comes alive at midnight.

Deep Read
Deep Read · The Ken + In The Money by Zerodha · 31 March 2026

Back in the day, gold lending in India was the moneylender's business. Walk in with your jewellery, get cash in minutes, pay usurious interest, pray you can repay. Regulated NBFCs changed that — but now the RBI is tightening the very rules that made the formalised sector attractive to private equity. And simultaneously, SEBI is formalising retail algo trading before the clock strikes midnight tonight.

The Bain-Manappuram story works like this. Gold is at all-time highs — $4,600/oz, ₹1,46,733 per 10 grams. Gold-loan NBFCs are growing at 25–30% annually. Every gram pledged unlocks more credit than it did three years ago, NPAs stay low because the collateral is liquid and easy to auction, and rural India has vast untapped gold sitting unmonetised in homes and temples. Bain Capital sees a structurally attractive, collateral-backed financial business with demographic tailwinds and an underserved rural customer base. It pays ₹4,385 crore — the largest PE bet ever in the sector — for a controlling stake in Manappuram Finance. The RBI, which has been watching the sector with concern since 2024 (flagging LTV violations, bullet repayment misuse, and agricultural loan end-use falsification), introduces its new framework effective April 1. The Ken's Mutasim Khan frames it simply: the regulator's new rules "undercut the country's largest private-equity bet on gold-lending to date." Bain's thesis is not broken — a 5–7 year holding period can absorb a regulatory cycle. But the first 18 months will test the deal assumptions that were baked in at the time of signing.

The SEBI algo story is different in character but equally significant in timing. From April 1, retail automated trading in India moves from a functional grey zone into a fully regulated category. API orders are now formally tagged at the exchange level. A static IP is mandatory for all API users. Market orders must include a protection parameter. Strategies above 10 orders per second need registration and audit. Zerodha's In The Money described this as the end of the "wild west" phase — and the beginning of something more formal, accountable, and ultimately scalable. The framework took 12 months to implement because brokers needed to rebuild infrastructure, not because rules changed. For the action checklist: static IP in Kite Connect settings, market protection parameter non-zero, strategy rate confirmed below 10/second. If you are a manual trader, nothing changes.

The Ken · PE · Gold
Bain vs the RBI — the full story of India's largest gold-lending PE bet and the regulatory freight train it didn't quite dodge
To make things easier to grasp, consider how gold loans actually work. A customer walks into a branch, pledges their jewellery, and receives cash at 65–75% of the gold's market value — in minutes, with minimal documentation. The NBFC holds the gold as collateral. If the customer doesn't repay, the NBFC auctions the gold. The system works because gold is liquid, globally priced, and never depreciates to zero. In a high-gold-price environment, the LTV buffer widens over time, making the portfolio safer even as it grows. Bain Capital's ₹4,385 crore bet on Manappuram Finance is built on this foundation: strong collateral quality, efficient collections, massive rural market, and gold prices supported by global uncertainty. The RBI's new April 1 framework introduces four key changes — (1) LTV caps enforced strictly, after audits found violations being gamed; (2) bullet repayment restrictions, ending the rollover-disguised-as-repayment product; (3) stricter auction norms, adding procedural steps to collateral sales; (4) end-use documentation for agricultural loans. Each change compresses the margin profile of the business Bain just bought. The structural thesis holds over time — India's 25,000+ tonnes of household gold are a generational monetisation opportunity. The near-term risk: if gold prices correct on an Iran ceasefire, collateral values shrink just as regulatory tightening is already biting. That combination, if it materialises, would test the deal thesis harder than either factor alone. Source: The Ken.

In The Money · SEBI · Algo
SEBI's algo framework — the complete action checklist for every API trader before markets open April 2
Back in the day — before 2025 — using APIs to automate trades in India was a functional grey zone. Zerodha's Kite Connect let retail traders place orders through code. It worked. But there was no formal regulatory structure governing who was accountable, how orders were identified, or what compliance was required. Vendors sold automated strategies to retail traders with aggressive performance claims and zero accountability. SEBI stepped in with a framework that took 12 months of infrastructure rebuilding to implement. From April 1, here is what changes: (1) API orders are formally tagged at the exchange level — every automated order is now traceable to its origin; (2) Static IP is mandatory for all API users — your code must originate from a fixed address, obtainable from your ISP (from ₹1,500/year), a cloud provider, or a VPS; (3) Market protection required — market orders must include a fill-price range, beyond which the order becomes a limit order; (4) Strategies above 10 orders per second require registration and audit through your broker. Below that threshold, standard compliance applies. Family and HUF accounts can share one developer account and static IP. Manual traders are completely unaffected. The practical checklist: add static IP in Kite Connect developer settings, confirm your market orders have a non-zero protection value, verify your strategy's order rate. That is it. The API and order flow don't change — just the compliance layer around them. Source: In The Money by Zerodha.

Iran · April 6 · Deadline
April 6: Trump's "major strikes" warning, WTI near $100, and India's rupee at ₹95.22. The two scenarios that will define FY27's first month.
The April 6 deadline is Trump's second ultimatum in two weeks. The first (March 22) was extended after "productive conversations." This one comes with a more specific warning: major strikes on Iranian energy infrastructure. The diplomatic track — Pakistan-mediated indirect talks, two Modi-Pezeshkian calls, and Jaishankar's ship-by-ship negotiation — has produced tangible results (Indian tankers transiting the strait), but not a structural resolution. WTI has climbed from $63 at the start of the war to nearly $100 today. For India, the two scenarios: a deal or credible ceasefire would cause a sharp oil selloff (potentially $20–30/barrel), rupee recovery from ₹95.22, and a strong FY27 opening rally. Strikes would push WTI toward $120–130, test the rupee at ₹100, and force the RBI into an emergency response. Goldman Sachs has India's 2026 GDP forecast at 5.9% — that number goes lower in the strikes scenario. Source: Aftermarket Report / multiple.

Global · Gold · Petroyuan
Gold at $4,600 is not just a safe-haven story — it is a petrodollar fragmentation story. And Finshots explained exactly why this week.
Gold's rise to $4,600/oz combines three forces: safe-haven demand from the Iran war, central bank diversification away from US dollar reserves (accelerated since Russia's 2022 sanctions showed the risk of dollar-denominated holdings), and real yield compression (energy inflation reduces real interest rates, the strongest structural predictor of gold prices). Monday's Finshots explained the petroyuan angle — Deutsche Bank research suggests the war could accelerate a shift from petrodollar to petroyuan as Gulf states question the US security guarantee that underpinned the original Saudi-dollar arrangement. China's case: it is the world's largest energy buyer since ~2016, has extended BRI loans across 150 oil-producing nations, and launched yuan crude oil futures in 2018. Reports of countries paying Iran in yuan getting smoother Hormuz passage add a tactical dimension. The likely outcome is not yuan replacing the dollar, but fragmentation — two currency blocs, higher transaction costs, more volatility. That environment structurally supports gold. Global · FY27 · Macro
The world FY27 opens into: US futures at 7-month lows, Japan bond yields at 1999 highs, Germany inflation at 2-year highs — and India at the centre of it all
The global macro backdrop as FY27 begins is the most complex in a decade. US equity futures ended last week at seven-month lows on Iran-war stagflation fears — rising energy prices compressing corporate margins while growth slows. Japan's 10-year bond yield is at 2.37%, its highest since 1999 — oil-driven inflation forcing the Bank of Japan's hand after two decades of near-zero rates. When Japanese yields rise, Japanese institutional investors (among the world's largest holders of foreign bonds) face a reduced incentive to hold US Treasuries and emerging market debt — a potential capital repatriation risk for Indian bond markets. Germany's inflation reached 2.7% in March, the highest since January 2024. South Korea's equity market fell 3% and the won hit a 17-year low as war fears spread. Against this backdrop, India's position is uncomfortable but not terminal: a war beneficiary of sorts in some sectors (domestic manufacturing, export competitiveness from the weak rupee) while simultaneously being one of the most exposed economies to the oil price shock. The April 6 deadline resolution — deal or strikes — will be the clearest binary event for global markets in months. Source: Aftermarket Report / multiple.
