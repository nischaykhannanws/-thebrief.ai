Earnings Lead · TCS Q4 FY26

TCS drops a blockbuster quarter into a weak market: profit +29% QoQ to ₹13,718 Cr, revenue ₹70,698 Cr, and AI revenues crossing $2.3B annualised for FY26.

Well, you see, the market opened weak and stayed there all session — a gap-down of 88 points tracking West Asia ceasefire doubts, sustained selling through the afternoon, and a modest recovery in the final hour that wasn't enough. Nifty closed at 23,775.10, down ~1%. 1,670 stocks declined on NSE versus 1,573 advances.

And then, after the close, TCS dropped a quarter that had nothing to do with the market's mood. Net profit came in at ₹13,718 Cr — up 29% sequentially. Revenue ₹70,698 Cr (+5.4% YoY). Margins at 25.3%. TCV (total contract value) of $12B for Q4 alone, $40.7B for the full FY26. Annualised AI revenues surpassed $2.3B. Attrition edged up to 13.7% LTM; TCS added 2,356 employees in Q4.

Deep Dive · Zerodha Daily Brief

The Hormuz crisis is reshaping the global energy transition — coal is back in Asia, EVs are surging in India, and nuclear is being reconsidered everywhere at once

The IEA called the Hormuz blockade the largest oil supply disruption in history. What's happened since is an energy paradox: the same crisis that's reviving coal in Japan and South Korea is accelerating EVs and solar in India and Vietnam, and pushing Taiwan and Japan toward nuclear reversals.

Japan lifted restrictions on older coal plants. South Korea removed its 80% cap on coal-fired generation. The catch: coal plants carry 30–40 year asset lifespans, so today's emergency becomes tomorrow's structural carbon commitment. India's story is different — it never made LNG a core bridge fuel (too expensive vs domestic coal), so its transition runs coal → renewables directly. India is now fast-tracking coal gasification as a domestic alternative while EV registrations jumped 50% YoY in March. Vietnam hit 38% EV sales share, Uruguay 27%, Indonesia 15% — all ahead of the US.

China's Oil Insulation

China imports less than 20% of its crude from any single source, holds a 108-day crude storage buffer, and its EV fleet displaced oil demand equivalent to what Saudi Arabia produces last year. Due to this, China is simultaneously the world's largest clean energy manufacturer and its largest coal consumer — and uniquely shielded from the Hormuz shock compared to every other major economy.

Zerodha Daily Brief · Gold
India built a gold exchange — nobody uses it. The ₹69B market still runs on phone calls between 16 banks and opaque bilateral pricing.
On April 1, India's DGFT restricted imports of gold, silver, and platinum jewellery with no grace period — because gold had become India's largest import after crude oil, hitting $14.72B in a single month in October 2025. The deeper story is how India's gold market actually works: 16 nominated banks (SBI, HDFC, ICICI etc.) control all gold imports on a consignment model. Prices are set bilaterally, over the phone, privately. India launched the IIBX exchange at GIFT City in 2022 — it handles only ~15% of imports. The 85% majority still flows through the opaque system because banks, foreign suppliers, and large dealers all benefit from the opacity.

Finshots · Aviation
IndiGo and Air India both lost their CEOs during a boom — the exits reveal what the next phase of Indian aviation actually demands
Pieter Elbers (IndiGo) and Campbell Wilson (Air India) stepped down within weeks of each other — not during a crisis but during record passenger traffic and the world's largest aircraft order wave. Finshots' read: India's Phase 1 aviation (routes, capacity, market share) is complete. IndiGo's incoming CEO Willie Walsh brings IAG (British Airways parent) experience navigating the 2008 fuel shock and complex multi-airline mergers — exactly the toolkit Phase 2 demands.

The Ken · Infrastructure
Meta's Project Waterworth lands at Visakhapatnam — connecting India to the US for the first time. The problem: India has no law protecting the cable.
Meta's Project Waterworth is the world's longest undersea internet cable. It will land at Rushikonda beach, Vizag, connecting India directly to the US, Brazil, and South Africa. Not only this — 95% of everything on the Indian internet already travels through undersea cables. India's $340B+ services-export economy depends on them. At Mumbai's Versova beach, where 6 of India's 17 international cables come ashore, there are no armed guards, no exclusion zones, and no law mandating cable protection. Low fences. Warning signs. Public beach access. India runs on infrastructure it has no legal framework to protect.
