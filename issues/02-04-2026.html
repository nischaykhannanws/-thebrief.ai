Lead · Aftermarket Report · Thursday 2 April

Nifty gapped down 296 points to 22,383, slid further to 22,180 by 10 AM, then completely reversed by close — a 2%+ intraday recovery that reminded everyone why the 11 AM panic-sell is rarely the right call

You see, today was one of those sessions that feel terrible in the morning and look like a gift by evening. Weak global cues — oil spiking past $107, Trump's Iran rhetoric rattling futures, US equity markets pulling back 1–1.5% overnight — meant Nifty opened with a 296-point gap-down to 22,383. It then slid further to around 22,180 in the first hour. Not a pleasant start.

Then, somewhere around 1:20 PM, the narrative shifted. Buyers stepped in — and they didn't stop. By close, Nifty was sitting at 22,713, having staged a full 500-point reversal from the session low. Nifty IT led sectoral gains at +2.60%, likely driven by the rupee's dramatic strengthening making export earnings more attractive on paper. Consumer Durables was the sole laggard, slipping 0.93%. Due to this broad recovery, of 3,311 stocks traded on NSE, 2,020 advanced against only 1,216 declines — a remarkably positive breadth given the gap-down open.

India's benchmark 10-year government bond yield climbed to 7.1% today — a level not seen since May 2024. The driver is straightforward: oil-driven inflation fears are making bond markets nervous. Due to this, rate hike whispers are getting louder among economists and bond dealers. The bond market is essentially pricing in a scenario where the RBI cannot cut rates, and may have to hike — a complete reversal from the easing narrative that dominated Q4 FY26.

Lead · Global Macro · Thursday 2 April

Oil crossed $107/bbl after Trump's speech signalled no quick end to Middle East hostilities — and a new front opened with proposed 100% tariffs on imported branded drugs under Section 232

Well, you see, the crude story today has two distinct drivers. First, the Iran premium: Trump's latest speech removed any expectation of a near-term diplomatic resolution, pushing WTI above $107/bbl. US equity futures pulled back 1–1.5% on escalation fears — which explains, in part, why Nifty gapped down so heavily this morning before recovering. The link between Middle East geopolitics and Indian equities remains direct and immediate.

Second, a new variable entered the frame: the US is reportedly considering tariffs of up to 100% on imported branded drugs under Section 232 of the Trade Expansion Act, if pricing deals with pharmaceutical companies cannot be reached. Indian pharma exporters — which derive a significant share of revenue from the US generics market — should watch this space extremely carefully. Branded drugs and generics are different segments, but regulatory creep across categories is the pattern to watch.

Europe · Fixed Income
Germany's 10-year Bund yield crossed 3% — its highest level since 2011, as inflation fears spread beyond Asia
The Bund yield breaking 3% is a significant global fixed income signal. Germany's benchmark bond had spent years below 2%, and its move above 3% signals that European markets are pricing in persistent inflation driven by energy costs — not just a transient spike. Not only this — a higher Bund yield compresses the risk premium available on emerging market debt, which can trigger portfolio outflows from markets like India. Due to this, the rupee's strength today becomes all the more important: if the Bund yield stays elevated, FPI flows into Indian bonds could face headwinds even if the RBI holds rates.

Trade · Indian Pharma
US may impose 100% tariffs on imported branded drugs under Section 232 — Indian pharma exporters on watch
The Trump administration is invoking Section 232 of the Trade Expansion Act — the same provision used to justify steel and aluminium tariffs on national security grounds — to threaten 100% tariffs on branded drug imports if pricing agreements are not reached. The stated logic: the US subsidises global drug R&D by paying above-market prices while other nations free-ride. Indian pharma exports to the US are predominantly generics, not branded drugs — but the sector's reaction will be worth watching. The risk is regulatory spillover or a broader reimagination of what counts as a "national security" import. Due to this, pharma heavyweights like Sun Pharma, Dr Reddy's, and Cipla warrant a close eye.

Commodities
Gold fell 4% to around $4,600 and silver dropped over 7% as the dollar strengthened on safe-haven flows
The conventional wisdom is that geopolitical turmoil drives gold higher. Today was a reminder that the dollar can override that logic. When risk escalates and the dollar strengthens sharply, gold — priced in dollars — often falls even as the underlying fear rises. That's precisely what happened today: dollar strength triggered by safe-haven flows pushed gold down 4% to ~$4,600, and silver — which has less monetary safe-haven status and more industrial demand sensitivity — fell over 7%. For Indian investors holding gold ETFs, the fall in USD gold price was partially offset by the strengthening rupee, making the net impact in INR terms more muted. Well, you see, currency moves rarely affect just one asset class at a time.

KitKats, cargo thieves, and a country that loses ₹12,000 crore a year before the goods even reach the shelf

4 reads
Lead · Finshots · Thursday 2 April

4 lakh KitKat bars stolen in transit between Italy and Poland — Finshots used this perfectly to explain why cargo theft is India's underrated logistics crisis

You see, a story about missing chocolate bars seems like it belongs in the quirky news sidebar. But Finshots turned it into one of the sharper economic explainers of the week. The key insight: food is the #1 stolen cargo category globally, accounting for 22% of all theft incidents — precisely because it lacks the traceability of electronics. No IMEI numbers. No remote lock capability. Just batch codes that buyers rarely check and supply chains that rarely flag missing pallets until the delivery shortfall shows up downstream.

The India angle is where it gets uncomfortable. India accounts for 63% of cargo theft cases in Asia, with annual losses estimated at nearly ₹12,000 crore. Not only is that a staggering headline number — it's a real drag on supply chain competitiveness at a time India is positioning itself as a global manufacturing and logistics hub. Due to this, rising cargo theft makes logistics more expensive, less predictable, and ultimately discourages the kind of large-scale inventory management that modern supply chains depend on.

The GDP angle is counterintuitive: stolen goods don't add to GDP because nothing new is produced. It's just a reshuffling of existing assets — formally recorded under "other changes in the volume of assets" in national accounts. The economy doesn't grow; it just rearranges who has the KitKats.

Energy Policy · India
E20 petrol — 20% ethanol blend — becomes India's new national default today, with 95 RON fuel as the standard
Starting today, E20 petrol (20% ethanol blend) is the new national default fuel standard, with 95 RON as the reference grade. This is a significant energy transition milestone — the culmination of India's ethanol blending programme that started with 5% targets and has progressively moved up. The implications are worth tracking: for vehicles calibrated for E20, performance should be unaffected or marginally improved. For older vehicles not calibrated for E20 — particularly older two-wheelers and carburettored engines — there could be performance implications including reduced mileage. Due to this, the rollout will require vehicle-wise assessment, and workshops across India are likely to see a rise in fuel-system queries in the coming months.
