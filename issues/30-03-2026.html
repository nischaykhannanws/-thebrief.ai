Aftermarket Report · Groww Digest · Monday 30 Mar 2026 · Last day of FY26

FY26 ended the way it deserved to — with a gap-down, a record-low rupee, bond yields crossing 7%, and Bank Nifty in freefall. Here is how India's last trading day of the financial year played out.

Lead · Aftermarket Report · Monday 30 March 2026

Nifty opened 270 points gap-down at 22,550, fell through 22,500 within the first hour, briefly pulled back to 22,600 by afternoon, then collapsed further to close at 22,331.40 — down 488 points (-2.14%). No sector closed green. PSU Banks fell 4.56%. Rupee hit ₹95.22 intraday. Bond yields crossed 7%. That is how FY26 ended.

You see, Monday's trigger was specific and came before markets even opened: the RBI announced it would cap banks' Net Open Positions in rupee forex (NOP-INR) at $100 million by April 10. Put simply, this means banks can no longer hold large unhedged short rupee positions in the forward markets — a move designed to reduce speculative pressure on the currency. The problem? Markets read it as confirmation that the RBI itself believes the rupee situation is serious enough to require emergency-style intervention. Bank Nifty fell nearly 4.56% in direct response.

The rest of the session unfolded from there. Nifty's intraday recovery attempt at 22,600 by 12:30 PM failed to hold, selling resumed, and the index slipped to 22,280 by 3 PM before a late partial recovery brought the close to 22,331.40. Only 4 Nifty stocks ended in the green: Hindalco (+2.05%), Coal India (+1.21%), ONGC (+0.96%), Power Grid (+0.20%). Everything else fell. Broader breadth was brutal: 570 advances vs 2,764 declines across NSE. As a close to FY26, this is a sobering verdict on a year that started with Nifty near 22,000 in April 2025, rallied toward 26,000 by September, and then spent the second half giving most of it back — ending almost exactly where it started, and in the worst week of the year.

Policy · RBI · Banks
RBI caps banks' net open rupee forex positions at $100mn — the most aggressive currency defence tool since 1998. Uday Kotak invokes Bimal Jalan.
The RBI's NOP-INR cap is not a routine policy adjustment. It is an emergency instrument. Back in the day, during the 1998 Asian currency crisis, RBI Governor Bimal Jalan deployed similar unconventional tools to halt a rupee freefall after the contagion from the Thai baht collapse hit India. The parallel Uday Kotak drew on Monday is deliberate and instructive. The NOP rule limits how much unhedged short-rupee exposure banks can carry at end of each business day. Banks had been using forward markets to speculate on further rupee weakness — which itself accelerates the decline. By capping positions, RBI is attacking the speculative feedback loop. Due to this, Bank Nifty fell 4.56% on the announcement — not because the rule hurts bank fundamentals, but because it reduces a profitable trading position and signals how seriously RBI views the situation. Kotak also floated the idea of a new FCNR(B) scheme — a foreign currency non-resident deposit product that the RBI used in 1998 and 2013 to attract dollar inflows from the Indian diaspora. If deployed, it would shore up forex reserves while incentivising NRI deposits. Source: Aftermarket Report.

Macro · Bonds
India's 10-year bond yield crossed 7% for the first time since July 2024 — what it means for government borrowing costs, EMIs, and FY27 budget math.
The 10-year government bond yield is the benchmark for borrowing costs across the entire economy. When it crossed 7% on Monday for the first time since July 2024, it sent a signal that markets are pricing in either higher inflation, higher fiscal deficit, or both — and that foreign capital is demanding more to stay invested in Indian assets. The mechanism: as WTI crude approaches $100 per barrel, India's import bill expands, the current account deficit widens, the rupee falls, and inflationary pressure builds. The RBI's inflation target framework (4% ±2%, just extended to 2031 by the government) means it cannot cut rates aggressively without risking credibility. Not only this — higher bond yields mean higher government interest payments on the ₹90+ lakh crore outstanding debt, which squeezes the fiscal space available for FY27 capital expenditure. For retail borrowers, bank lending rates are closely tied to government yield movements. EMI relief — which many had hoped for from RBI rate cuts in FY27 — becomes less likely the longer yields stay elevated. Source: Aftermarket Report / Daily Brief.

Stocks · Metals
Hindalco, NALCO, Vedanta surged as aluminium hits 4-year highs — Iran-linked attacks on Middle East smelters are reshaping global aluminium supply
On a day when almost everything fell, Hindalco (+2.05%), NALCO, and Vedanta's aluminium business bucked the trend sharply. The reason: aluminium prices surged toward four-year highs after reports that Iran-linked attacks had hit Middle East smelters — industrial aluminium production facilities in the Gulf region. Aluminium smelting is an extremely energy-intensive process, requiring vast quantities of cheap electricity. Gulf smelters have traditionally benefited from cheap gas-derived power. The combination of direct attack damage and gas supply disruption is reducing Gulf aluminium output significantly. Not only this — the Iran war has also constrained alumina (refined bauxite) shipping logistics through Hormuz, tightening raw material supply for smelters globally. India's aluminium producers — Hindalco (Novelis globally), NALCO, and Vedanta's Jharsuguda plant — are primary beneficiaries: they use domestically sourced coal-based power and are unaffected by Hormuz disruption, making them globally competitive at current price levels. Due to this, Indian aluminium stocks are emerging as one of the cleaner structural beneficiary plays of the Iran war — a theme worth tracking as Hormuz remains disrupted. ETAuto · Monday 30 Mar 2026 · FY26 Auto Review

India's auto industry just closed FY26 with all-time highs — despite a war, a record fuel price, and a rupee in freefall. CAFE-2 penalties just got smaller. And PM E-Drive incentives just got four more months.

3 stories
Lead · ETAuto · Monday 30 March 2026

Indian auto industry sales in FY26 hit all-time highs. This is a remarkable statement to make on the last day of a financial year defined by an oil war, a record-low currency, and fuel prices at their highest in history. And yet, the data is unambiguous: both overall volumes and EV penetration ended the year at record levels, led by an extraordinary March that saw electric two-wheeler sales hit their highest monthly number ever.

Well, you see, the India auto story in FY26 has two distinct halves. The first half (April–September 2025) was conventional strong: pent-up demand, strong rural recovery, and steady urban income growth drove volume. The second half (October 2025–March 2026) should have been catastrophic — but wasn't. Fuel prices spiked to their highest ever. The rupee weakened 10%. Consumer confidence took a hit. And yet, volume held. The explanation: EV adoption accelerated precisely because petrol became unaffordable. The middle-lane (₹10–30 lakh) electric car segment was the fastest-growing in FY26. PM E-Drive scheme incentives supported 2W EV adoption through March, and the government's decision to extend these incentives by four months to July 2026 ensures the momentum carries into FY27.

The CAFE-2 penalty revision is separately significant. Corporate Average Fuel Economy (CAFE-2) norms set mandatory fleet fuel efficiency targets for automakers. Nine OEMs — including several major players — had been facing a combined ₹5,000+ crore penalty for not meeting these targets. The Ministry revised the calculation methodology to allow surplus credits to be pooled within a manufacturer group, bringing the total penalty down to ₹2,728 crore. This is meaningful relief for companies like Maruti Suzuki and Hyundai whose product mix has been skewed toward larger, less fuel-efficient vehicles in recent years — though the direction of travel on efficiency norms remains unchanged.

Policy · CAFE-2
CAFE-2 penalty on 9 carmakers revised down to ₹2,728 crore — how surplus credit pooling changed the maths
CAFE (Corporate Average Fuel Economy) norms require automakers to ensure their entire fleet sold in a year meets a minimum average fuel efficiency standard. The CAFE-2 norms (effective from FY22) are stricter than CAFE-1, requiring manufacturers to improve fleet efficiency significantly. Back in the day, each model was measured individually and penalties calculated per model shortfall. The revised methodology allows surplus credits from efficient models (like EVs, CNG vehicles, small hatchbacks) to be pooled across a manufacturer's entire India portfolio. This is important because companies like Maruti have a wide product mix — their Swift and Alto generate large efficiency surpluses that can now offset shortfalls from Brezza, Grand Vitara, and Jimny. The result: the combined penalty across 9 OEMs drops from an estimated ₹5,000+ crore to ₹2,728 crore. Not only this — the revision also reduces the risk of further non-compliance in FY27 for manufacturers who are rapidly adding EVs and CNG models to their portfolios. The government's intent is to incentivise fleet electrification rather than purely punish non-compliance. Source: ETAuto.

Policy · EV
PM E-Drive extended 4 months to July 2026 + India's electric 2W sales hit an all-time monthly high in March — the incentive is working exactly as intended
PM E-Drive (Electric Drive India) is the current government scheme providing purchase subsidies for electric two-wheelers, replacing the earlier FAME-II scheme. The scheme was set to expire March 31, 2026 — today. The government has extended it by four months to July 31, 2026 with the same incentive structure intact. The timing is deliberate: March 2026 just recorded India's all-time monthly high for electric 2W sales, driven by the subsidy deadline creating a pull-forward effect and petrol prices at record highs making the economics of EV commuting compelling. Not only this — the Iran war has created a structural rather than temporary argument for EVs in India. When petrol costs ₹110–120 per litre and the rupee is at ₹95/dollar, the annual fuel cost differential between a petrol scooter and an electric scooter approaches ₹25,000–40,000 depending on usage. That is a material number for India's price-sensitive two-wheeler buyer. Due to this, extending the E-Drive scheme ensures the demand momentum from March carries into the summer months — historically a seasonally strong period for 2W purchases. Source: ETAuto.

Industry · FY26 Review
FY26 auto sales analysis: how India's auto industry hit all-time highs during a war, a currency crisis, and the highest fuel prices in history
To put things into perspective, consider what FY26 threw at the Indian auto industry: oil war starting February 28 driving WTI from $63 to near $100 in four weeks; rupee weakening 10% to record lows; retail petrol prices at their highest ever; consumer confidence surveys at multi-year lows; FII outflows creating wealth destruction in equity-linked assets. Social media is facing its biggest legal reckoning since the internet was invented. The petrodollar's 50-year reign may be cracking. And a European royal family lost a billion dollars betting on Indian startups.

3 reads
Lead · The Daily Brief (Zerodha) · Monday 30 March 2026

Two American juries delivered verdicts against Meta within 48 hours of each other last week — and neither case was about what Meta's users posted. Both were about how Meta engineered its platforms to trap people inside them. That distinction is the most significant legal shift in the history of the internet.

You see, for nearly three decades, social media companies have been shielded by a single law: Section 230 of the US Communications Decency Act (1996). The law made platforms legal non-publishers — they were just conduits for other people's speech. If someone posted something harmful, the liability rested with the poster, not the platform. This was the foundational legal bargain that allowed Facebook, YouTube, Instagram, and TikTok to grow without fear of being crushed by lawsuits for user content. Back in the day, this made sense: early internet platforms genuinely couldn't monitor everything posted by millions of users. The trade-off was considered fair. Well, let's find out why it no longer is.

The New Mexico case: investigators posed as children under 14 on Instagram and were contacted by adults almost immediately. A jury ordered $375 million in penalties — not for the content, but for Meta's decision to run an algorithm that facilitated those contacts. The LA case: Meta and YouTube were found to have negligently designed platforms that trap teenagers in addictive spirals. Mark Zuckerberg testified for two full days. The shift in both cases is from content liability (what was posted) to product liability (how the platform is built). This is the same legal theory that makes car manufacturers responsible for seatbelts and pharmaceutical companies for side effect warnings. You are held responsible for the foreseeable consequences of how you design your product — not just what it contains. Not only this — the legal shift is global. EU's Digital Services Act, UK Online Safety Act, Brazil's ECA Digital law: all moving toward "safety-by-design" standards. India is still in the older content-specific framework, but a parliamentary committee report tabled this month recommends requiring platforms to meet safety-by-design standards for mental health of young users. The first crack in the wall is visible.

Daily Brief · Rupee
The Daily Brief's rupee deep-dive: why India's currency fell 10% in a year — and most of it happened before the war even started
The rupee's weakness is not a simple oil shock story. A year ago, the exchange rate was ₹85.5/dollar. By the time the Iran war began on February 28, 2026, it had already slid to ₹91 — a 6.5% decline that happened entirely in peacetime, driven by persistent FII outflows, India's current account deficit, and the strong dollar globally. The war then accelerated what was already happening: in under four weeks, the rupee went from ₹91 to past ₹95 — a near-vertical move on the chart. According to MUFG Bank research, framing this as purely an oil story misses India's four other channels of Hormuz exposure. Channel one: LPG cannot be rerouted like crude. Most of India's LPG comes from the Gulf via specialised refrigerated tankers; the global spot market for it is thin and infrastructure tied to Gulf sources. Channel two: approximately 60% of India's LNG imports transit Hormuz — mostly from Qatar. Petronet invoked force majeure on March 3. Channel three: natural gas is the primary raw material for urea fertiliser; cut gas supply and domestic urea production falls. Plus 40% of India's finished fertiliser imports come from the Gulf — a shock that will only show up fully when kharif sowing begins in June. Channel four: remittances — around 30% of all Indian remittances from overseas come from Gulf workers; if earnings there are disrupted, those dollar inflows shrink. MUFG estimates every $10 rise in oil prices widens India's current account deficit by 0.4–0.5% of GDP. The REER (Real Effective Exchange Rate) fell from 102.3 in February 2025 to 94.1 in February 2026 — meaning Indian assets are currently on sale. The RBI's own March Bulletin candidly notes that its analysis "does not cover the full impact of the Middle East conflict." That is the institution responsible for managing the rupee telling you the worst is not yet formally measured. Source: Daily Brief (Zerodha).

Finshots · Global Finance
Finshots: The rise of the petroyuan — is the dollar's 50-year grip on oil trade finally cracking under the weight of this war?
Back in the early 1970s, Saudi Arabia signed a series of informal agreements with the US: sell oil in US dollars, recycle the surplus into US assets, and receive military protection in return. This petrodollar arrangement was the foundation of dollar dominance in global trade for 50 years. The system didn't just cover oil — it anchored the dollar as the world's reserve currency, gave the US cheap borrowing, and let it run persistent deficits without the usual currency punishment. Well, according to new Deutsche Bank research, the Iran war may be the turning point that finally cracks this arrangement. The argument runs like this: Gulf states signed up for petrodollars in exchange for US military protection. But the US hasn't protected Gulf oil assets from being targeted in this war. Why should they maintain their end of the deal? China, meanwhile, has been the world's largest energy buyer since around 2016 (after the US shale boom reduced US import dependence). It has deployed Belt and Road Initiative loans to oil-producing nations, creating yuan leverage. In 2018 it launched yuan crude oil futures convertible to gold — giving sellers an alternative to holding yuan. Reports suggest countries paying Iran in yuan are getting smoother Hormuz passage. That said, the petroyuan replacing the petrodollar outright is still far-fetched. China tightly controls its currency; yuan isn't freely usable internationally; and the gold-convertibility mechanism has Bretton Woods-style limits. The more likely outcome, Finshots argues, is fragmentation: two currency blocs settling different oil trades in different currencies, raising transaction costs, adding volatility, and heightening geopolitical tension. That is a worse outcome than either dollar dominance or clean yuan dominance — it is the worst-case scenario for global trade efficiency. Source: Finshots.

The Ken · Monday 30 March 2026

India's AI copyright problem has three bodies and no clean solution. The Ken maps the tension between creators, AI companies, and the public good — and why the commerce ministry's proposal satisfies none of them fully.

Deep Read
Deep Read · The Ken · Monday 30 March 2026

You have probably used an AI tool to write, summarise, or analyse something this week. What you may not have considered is whether the authors, journalists, and creators whose work trained that AI model have any right to compensation — or even to say no. India's commerce ministry just tabled a proposal on exactly this question. And the answer, as The Ken explains, is more tangled than it seems.

Well, let's find out. The AI copyright problem has what The Ken calls a "three-body problem" structure — three competing interests that cannot all be satisfied simultaneously. Body one: creators. Authors, journalists, musicians, and other content producers whose work has been scraped and used to train AI models without their consent or compensation. Their position: this is a violation of copyright, full stop. Body two: AI companies. OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, and their Indian equivalents argue that training on publicly available text is fair use — that reading and learning from human-created content is transformative in the legal sense, and that restricting it would cripple AI development. Body three: the public interest. Society benefits from better AI tools — in medicine, education, productivity, accessibility. Overly protecting creator rights could slow AI development in ways that hurt everyone, including creators themselves.

India's commerce ministry panel has proposed a "blanket consent with royalties" model: AI companies get a statutory licence to use copyrighted material for training, but must pay into a royalty pool that distributes back to creators. This sounds elegant — and there are global precedents in music (where radio stations pay blanket licensing fees to play any song). But as The Ken explains, the music precedent breaks down quickly. Music has established collective rights organisations (IPRS in India), standardised royalty rates, and a manageable number of rights holders. AI training datasets contain trillions of words from millions of creators across dozens of countries with different copyright regimes, different languages, and no equivalent of a collective rights organisation. Who decides what each word is worth? How do you verify which specific texts were used in training? How do you prevent AI companies from simply moving their training data centres to more permissive jurisdictions? The three-body problem has no stable equilibrium — every solution that satisfies one body destabilises another. Source: The Ken.

Energy · Bypass Routes
Saudi Arabia's pipeline is pumping 7 million barrels per day bypassing Hormuz — the world's largest oil bypass effort, and why it still leaves a 14mn bpd gap
Saudi Arabia has dramatically expanded operations on its East-West Pipeline (known as the Petroline), which runs from its eastern oil fields to the Red Sea port of Yanbu, completely bypassing the Strait of Hormuz. The pipeline is now pumping approximately 7 million barrels per day — near its theoretical maximum capacity. The UAE has a smaller bypass pipeline to its port of Fujairah on the Gulf of Oman, carrying over 1.5 million barrels per day. Together, that's roughly 8–9 million barrels per day of bypass capacity. The problem: the Strait of Hormuz was carrying approximately 20 million barrels per day before the crisis. The gap — roughly 14 million barrels per day from Iraq, Kuwait, Qatar, and Bahrain that have no bypass infrastructure at all — has no near-term solution. Iraq's entire export infrastructure flows through the Gulf. Qatar's LNG exports have no meaningful pipeline alternatives. Kuwait's oil reaches Hormuz or doesn't reach global markets at all. CCI clears MUFG's ₹39,618 crore stake in Shriram Finance — the largest FDI deal in India's financial sector, despite the war
The Competition Commission of India approved MUFG Bank's ₹39,618 crore investment for a 20% stake in Shriram Finance — making it the largest foreign direct investment deal ever in India's financial sector. MUFG (Mitsubishi UFJ Financial Group) is Japan's largest bank and one of the world's biggest. The deal is notable for several reasons. First, its scale: ₹39,618 crore (~$4.2 billion) is a significant bet on India's NBFC (non-banking financial company) sector even in a period of macroeconomic stress. Second, the target: Shriram Finance is India's largest retail NBFC, primarily serving MSME and commercial vehicle borrowers — segments MUFG has identified as structurally underpenetrated by formal banking. Third, the timing: committing this capital during an active oil war and currency crisis signals long-term conviction in India's financial sector growth story, even if the near-term macro is turbulent. The deal will boost Shriram's capital base, lower its cost of funds (MUFG's strong credit rating helps Shriram access cheaper international borrowing), and help it compete more aggressively in gold loans, MSME, and retail lending.
