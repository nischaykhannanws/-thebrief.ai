Nifty closes near 23,000 as breadth surges — RBI rate cut expectation and easing crude drive Monday's recovery

5 stories
Lead · Aftermarket Report · Monday

Gap-up open, an early dip to 22,560, then a sharp post-noon breakout. Nifty closed at 22,968 — near 23,000 — with 2,511 stocks advancing and 11 of 12 sectors in the green.

Well, you see, Monday was a textbook RBI-anticipation session. Nifty opened gap-up at 22,780 but sold off in the first hour to approximately 22,560 as early traders took profits from Friday's positioning. Then, right through 12:30 PM, a sharp breakout pushed the index decisively higher and it held those gains into the close at 22,968.25. The breadth story is perhaps more significant than the headline number: 2,511 stocks advanced versus only 746 declined out of 3,346 traded. Consumer Durables led all sectors with a 2.60% gain. Only Media ended in the red, and just barely, at -0.22%.

Not only this — the catalyst is clearly front-running of an RBI rate cut. Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman explicitly flagged that the RBI has room to cut interest rates and provide targeted support to sectors affected by the Middle East conflict. That statement, in the context of oil retreating slightly from its highs (Brent $108.39, down 0.6%), gave markets the confidence to push near resistance. The OI picture is instructive: Max Call OI sits at 23,200 (resistance zone 23,100–23,200), Max Put OI at 22,500. The market is ranged — the RBI's tone on Wednesday will determine whether 23,200 is broken or whether 22,700 reasserts.

Banking · Results
HDFC Bank Q4: Advances +17% YoY to ₹25L crore — but C-D ratio at 106–108% flags a liquidity stress that won't resolve quickly
HDFC Bank's Q4 numbers show strong loan growth — advances up 17% year-on-year to ₹25 lakh crore — but deposits at ₹23.5 lakh crore are not keeping pace. The resulting credit-to-deposit ratio of 106–108% is elevated beyond comfortable territory and signals tight CASA (Current and Savings Account) growth. Well, you see, a C-D ratio above 100% means the bank is lending out more than it holds in deposits — which means it is relying on bulk, higher-cost borrowings to fund growth. Due to this, net interest margins are under pressure in coming quarters even if the loan book grows. Source: Aftermarket Report.

Ceasefire signals in West Asia, Israel restores Leviathan gas, and JPMorgan's Jamie Dimon warns of a systemic oil shock

3 reads
Global Macro · Monday 6 April

Gold has pulled back, crude is slightly softer — markets are pricing in some ceasefire probability. But Jamie Dimon's quarterly letter says the baseline assumption is wrong.

Well, you see, the macro signals today are deliberately ambiguous. On the positive side: Israel has restarted the Leviathan and Tamar gas fields and restored approximately 1.1 billion cubic feet per day of gas exports to Egypt. Gold fell below $4,700 on ceasefire optimism — a meaningful signal, since gold had climbed on war risk. Crude retreated slightly. These are all "risk-on, war-off" moves.

But Jamie Dimon's letter to JPMorgan shareholders this morning is a striking counterpoint. He warned explicitly: because of the war in Iran, the bank "additionally faces the potential for significant ongoing oil and commodity price shocks" that "may lead to stickier inflation and ultimately higher interest rates than markets currently expect." Dimon's framing matters because it is not about today's ceasefire rumour — it is about the structural uncertainty that persists even if fighting pauses. Due to this, markets may be mispricing the duration risk. Finance Minister Sitharaman described the Middle East conflict as having "evolved into a systemic tremor threatening vital arteries of global energy" — language that aligns exactly with Dimon's concern.

The economics of the Moon, CNG as India's unlikely EV bridge, and what your missed SIP is really costing you

4 reads
Lead · Zerodha Daily Brief · Monday

India's mandatory CSR law mobilised ₹1.22 lakh crore since FY14. But the academic evidence says it increased the quantity of giving while making companies riskier, cutting off the best-placed NGOs, and turning the floor into a ceiling.

Well, you see, Section 135 of the Companies Act — India's mandatory 2% CSR rule — is a genuine global first. No other major economy has replicated it. And on its face, it is a policy success: ₹1.22 lakh crore mobilised, ₹19,000 crore in FY24 alone, two-thirds of companies voluntarily spending above the mandated floor. ₹7,000 crore deployed during COVID. Real money, real causes.

But the Zerodha Daily Brief's research-backed deep dive surfaces two structural problems and three perverse academic findings. The structural problems: Maharashtra gets 34% of all CSR spend and the top 10 states absorb 95%, while the government's most deprived "aspirational districts" received just 12% in FY24. The "local area preference" clause directs money where industry already sits — not where need is highest. And the share of companies using external NGOs (who had the community relationships to deploy money effectively) collapsed from 78% in FY20 to just 28% today, replaced by in-house foundations.

The academic findings are more damning. Companies that were previously generous — spending around 10.8% of profits on CSR — cut back to 3.6% after the law passed. The mandate destroyed the signalling value of generosity: why spend more when everyone's forced to spend something? The 2% floor became the ceiling. Not only this — mandatory CSR converted a variable cost into a quasi-fixed obligation, raising systematic risk (beta) by approximately 8% for mandated firms. And the law's announcement caused a 4.1% drop in shares of affected companies. As a side note: companies near the ₹5 crore profit threshold inflated R&D expenditure to stay below the CSR trigger — accidentally generating more patents as a byproduct of tax avoidance.

Finshots · Space Economics
Finshots on NASA's Artemis: the Moon costs $93 billion so far and there's no near-term revenue model — but the economics are more interesting than you think
Finshots' Monday piece frames NASA's Artemis programme through an economic lens rather than a scientific one. Apollo cost approximately $26 billion ($300B+ inflation-adjusted). Artemis has cost $93 billion so far — and Artemis II is a crewed lunar flyby, not even a landing. So what justifies the cost? NASA's answer rests on three pillars: the Moon as a test environment for deep-space systems (radiation, life-support, logistics); water ice near the lunar south pole (transporting water from Earth costs $22,000 per litre — the Moon could be a refuelling station); and permanent infrastructure that reduces future mission costs. The headline resource is Helium-3 — potential use in clean nuclear fusion, quantum computing, and medical imaging, with millions per kilogram in value — but extraction technology doesn't exist at scale yet. The conclusion is honest: no near-term revenue model, possible $1 trillion in sustained infrastructure costs, but a real (not purely geopolitical) attempt to build long-run economic viability for off-world presence. The Ken · Fintech
Your missed SIP is making banks tens of crores a month — and MP Raghav Chadha called it "legalised pickpocketing"
With 90 million SIP accounts in India, even small failure rates generate enormous penalty revenue. A missed SIP triggers a ₹500 bounce charge plus 18% GST — framed as a cost recovery fee by banks. The Ken's story by Mutasim Khan adds a layer beyond the minimum balance penalty story: SIP bounce fees are a separate and growing revenue stream. In March, MP Raghav Chadha flagged that banks collected ₹19,000 crore over three years purely as minimum balance penalties. Due to this structure, SIP penalties are a hidden tax on India's retail investor base — penalty structures that bear no relationship to actual bank costs or risk, compounding into thousands of crores annually at scale. Clean mobility at 30%, Karnataka's contradictory EV tax, Classic Legends in India's top 10, and CNG prices rising on war costs

4 stories
Lead · ETAuto · Monday

A landmark FY26 data point: EVs, CNG, and hybrids together captured nearly 30% of India's auto market — driven by fuel price volatility, government push, and growing model availability.

Well, you see, 30% is not a headline that arrives quietly. For a market that was still single-digit on clean mobility shares as recently as FY22, crossing 30% in FY26 is a structural shift — not a cyclical blip. The driver mix matters: EVs are growing but still a smaller share; CNG is doing the heavy lifting in the short term (as The Ken's story above explains); and strong hybrid adoption — particularly Toyota's self-charging hybrid lineup — is filling the gap between petrol and full EV.

The policy context is messier. The same week this data lands, Karnataka has deepened a controversy over a new lifetime tax on EVs — one that Union Minister Pralhad Joshi fears will derail India's emission targets. Contradictorily, Karnataka is also planning EV charging stations every 60–70 km on state highways. Due to this, FY26's 30% clean mobility share may mask the structural fragility of the policy environment that got it there. The GST cut-driven surge in car sales earlier in H2 FY26 is also expected to create a base effect that decelerates growth in FY27. Tata Motors led PV recovery post-GST cut, but analysts are already flagging affordability concerns for next year.

EV Policy · State
Karnataka's EV tax is contradicting its own charging infra push — and it could derail India's emission targets, warns Union Minister
Karnataka has introduced a lifetime tax on electric vehicles — the same state that is simultaneously planning EV charging stations every 60–70 km on state highways. Union Minister Pralhad Joshi has warned that the EV tax will derail India's national emission targets by removing the cost advantage that drives EV adoption. Well, you see, this is a textbook case of state fiscal interests conflicting with national policy. State governments lose motor vehicle tax revenue as ICE vehicles are replaced by EVs, creating an incentive to levy equivalent taxes on EVs. But the entire EV value proposition for buyers rests on lower running costs — including lower upfront tax. Due to this, Karnataka's move, if replicated by other states, could meaningfully slow the pace of EV adoption that the 30% clean mobility headline above was celebrating. Source: ETAuto.

Zerodha · SME Economics
Informal sector competition is driving frugal innovation in Indian SMEs — firms near informal rivals respond with "innovation time" for employees
Also in the Zerodha Daily Brief's CSR edition: a research finding on how informal sector competition drives frugal innovation in Indian SMEs. Firms facing pressure from informal rivals — who don't pay taxes or follow labour rules — respond by giving employees discretionary "innovation time," producing process improvements rather than technology R&D. The mechanism is unexpected: companies can't match informal rivals on cost, so they out-innovate them instead. Due to this, India's informal sector may inadvertently be functioning as an innovation pressure system for formal SMEs — a structural dynamic that plays out alongside, not because of, India's formal R&D ecosystem.
