Lead · Aftermarket Report · Tuesday 7 Apr

A gap-down open at 22,839 on weak global cues — but buyers stepped in all session and pushed Nifty to close near session highs at 23,123. 10 of 12 sectors ended green. IT led the recovery at +2.50%.

Well, you see, the setup this morning looked nasty. Global cues were soft — yen weakening toward ¥160/$, Chinese FX reserves posting their sharpest monthly drop in a decade, and Brent near $109. Nifty opened gap-down at 22,839. And then something interesting happened: the buyers didn't wait. Steady accumulation through the morning session pushed the index higher all day, closing near session highs in the 23,100–23,150 zone at 23,123.

Not only this — the breadth was genuinely constructive. 2,082 stocks advanced vs 1,149 declining out of 3,314. Nifty IT's +2.50% was the standout, partly explained by Samsung's 8x profit jump on AI memory chip demand giving the entire tech supply chain a bid. PSU Banks were the lone weak spot (−0.71%), which makes sense: the market is pricing in uncertainty on the RBI's INR management vs. liquidity trade-off ahead of tomorrow's policy decision. Due to this, the single most important event for tomorrow is not a macro number — it is a policy call. Does the RBI prioritise INR stability or ease liquidity? That answer drops tomorrow morning.

Brent near $109/bbl as markets await the US-Iran Hormuz deadline — two scenarios, very different consequences
Markets are pricing two outcomes on the Hormuz deadline: a negotiated off-ramp (Brent falls toward $85–90) or escalation (crude pushes well past $120). 4 reads
Finshots · Finance Deep Dive

$1.8 trillion in private credit — and the first serious crack: Blue Owl capped withdrawals at 5% when investors tried to pull 20%+ out of a $36B fund

Private credit — lending done by non-bank asset managers like Blue Owl, BlackRock, Ares, Apollo, and KKR — has grown five times since 2008 to approximately $1.8 trillion globally. For a long time this looked like an unambiguously good story: higher yields for investors, flexible credit for companies, and no systemic exposure because it sits outside the regulated banking system.

Well, you see, the cracks are now visible. Blue Owl Capital (managing $300B+ in assets) capped investor withdrawals at just 5% when redemption requests for its $36B private credit pool hit 20%+. Multiple other major firms are doing the same. The trigger is what Finshots calls the "SaaSpocalypse" — agentic AI fears have spooked investors in mid-sized SaaS companies, which make up about 20% (~$500B) of all private credit lending. Morgan Stanley warns default rates could climb to 8% versus the normal 2–2.5%. Not only this — these are long-duration loans. Fund managers can't demand early repayment, and the secondary market is thin; buyers are demanding heavy discounts. Due to this, what looked like a contained private asset class is starting to show systemic contours. The cracks actually started in September 2025 when Tricolor and First Brands Group first went bust — as Jamie Dimon put it: "When you see one cockroach, there's probably more."

Zerodha Daily Brief · PSU Policy
India's PSU Ratna system has never downgraded a single PSU — MTNL is still a Navratna despite ₹3,300 Cr losses
The Maharatna/Navratna/Miniratna system gives autonomous PSUs real capex freedom — up to ₹5,000 Cr per project for Maharatnas. But it only rewards winners. Not one PSU has ever been downgraded. MTNL retains Navratna status despite massive losses. 441 of 750 independent director slots are vacant. Ministerial overrides are routine. The Somanathan Committee reviewed it, FY27 budget came and went — no changes.

Zerodha Daily Brief · Tax Policy
GST was supposed to self-police through ITC — three design choices broke that mechanism and now India taxes production
GST's self-enforcement relies on buyers demanding invoices to claim input tax credits. But blocked credits lists, narrow refund rules, and a supplier-default penalty that hits buyers have broken the loop. The result: India's consumption tax effectively taxes production at every stage, making Indian manufacturers less competitive than importers who pay clean GST at the border. China fixed this between 2009 and 2016. India has the infrastructure. It lacks the political will.

The Ken · Auto & Climate
Maruti and Tata, squeezed by war, EV delays, and emission rules, find an unlikely fix — ethanol blending at 19% lower CO₂
The triple squeeze — Hormuz-driven fuel costs, EV adoption delays, and tightening emission norms — is pushing both carmakers toward higher ethanol blend mandates. Ethanol emits 19% less CO₂ than petrol, costs less than imported oil, and is domestically produced. It also has political alignment: the sugarcane industry (politically powerful in Maharashtra and UP) benefits from higher offtake. Both OEMs are quietly lobbying to accelerate the E20 mandate and push toward E30.

FY26 closes at a record 2.97 crore units — two-wheelers surge 13.4%, Tata nearly triples mid-SUV sales, and the EV story splits

5 items
Lead · ETAuto · FY26 Full Year Retail

FY26 auto retail hits a record 2.97 crore units. Two-wheelers at 2.14 crore (+13.4%). Passenger vehicles up 13% to 47.1 lakh. FADA: March was the best-ever monthly sale on record.

Well, you see, FY26 is the year Indian auto retail crossed a threshold nobody was certain it would reach. Total retail hit 2.97 crore units — the highest ever. Two-wheelers drove volume at 2.14 crore units (+13.4% YoY), benefiting from rural demand recovery, strong financing availability, and the shift back to personal mobility. Passenger vehicles rose 13% to 47.1 lakh units — a number that looked aggressive at the start of FY26 but came in just on target.

Not only this — March itself was FADA's best-ever recorded monthly retail. The end-of-fiscal push, pre-price-hike buying incentive (multiple OEMs announced April increases), and a strong wedding season all converged. Due to this, FY27 now has a high base to beat — particularly in PVs where the mid-cycle slowdown risk is real if West Asia keeps fuel prices elevated through the first two quarters.

Hormuz crisis halts Sojat's ₹4,000–5,000 Cr annual henna trade — ₹250 Cr stuck at ports, production down 80%
Sojat in Rajasthan accounts for ~90% of global natural henna exports. The Gulf is its primary market. Hormuz closure has effectively frozen the trade: ₹250 Cr stuck at ports, production down 80%, and tens of thousands of farm households facing acute income disruption. The macroeconomic aggregate misses this — but the household-level impact is severe. Geopolitical events have very uneven distributional consequences within India's economy.
